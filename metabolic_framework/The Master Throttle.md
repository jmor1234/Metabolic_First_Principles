> [!abstract] Essence
> T3 is the variable that sets how fast the entire metabolic system runs — uniquely among hormones, it simultaneously regulates both the nuclear genome (most of the ~100 electron-transport-chain subunits) and the mitochondrial genome (the remaining 13, via the p43 receptor encoded by the same c-erbA-alpha gene), so it directly determines the ceiling on oxidative capacity itself.
> Because of this, most problems that appear independently caused — elevated cholesterol, progesterone deficiency and estrogen dominance, serotonin release (ATP falling → V-ATPase failing), slow reflexes, low temperature, failing hearts — are downstream consequences of insufficient T3, and many self-correct once the throttle is restored.
> Hypothyroidism is self-sustaining because its suppressors (PUFA, estrogen, cortisol, endotoxin) act multiplicatively rather than additively — each accumulates precisely because T3 is too low to clear it, and each in turn further suppresses T3 — so breaking the cycle requires addressing multiple suppressors simultaneously alongside the three non-negotiable mineral cofactors (iodine as substrate, selenium for deiodinase catalytic sites, zinc for TPO expression).
> The standard diagnostic system measures a pituitary signal (TSH) and a prohormone (T4) while excluding the active hormone (T3) — producing simultaneous mass overtreatment and systematic undertreatment (most starkly, the 12-30% DIO2 polymorphism carriers for whom T4-only therapy is inherently insufficient regardless of dose) — which is why functional readouts (temperature, pulse, ETCO₂, reflex relaxation) are needed alongside blood tests to detect the T3-deficient-in-tissues patient whose numbers look "normal."

Every process described in this vault runs at a rate. That rate is set by triiodothyronine — T3.

[[Foundation]] established that health is efficient oxidative metabolism — primarily of glucose — to CO2, and that every cellular function requires ATP. [[The System]] documented how the components that produce and consume ATP form self-reinforcing loops. But neither document names the variable that determines how fast the entire system runs. T3 is that variable. It controls the expression of the respiratory enzymes that produce ATP — and it does so through a mechanism no other hormone uses: simultaneous regulation of both the nuclear and mitochondrial genomes. The nuclear genome encodes most of the ~100 subunits of the electron transport chain. The mitochondrial genome encodes 13 essential subunits. T3 controls both. No other signaling molecule coordinates transcription across both genomes.

This makes T3 the master throttle. When it is adequate, mitochondria are assembled, respiratory capacity is high, and the downstream cascade — steroid hormone synthesis, estrogen clearance, CO2 production, gut motility, neurotransmitter balance — operates. When it is inadequate, every one of those processes slows, and the system drifts toward the lactate-dominant attractor described in [[The System]]. Many chronic problems that appear to have independent causes — high cholesterol, estrogen dominance, serotonin excess, gut stagnation, progesterone deficiency — are downstream consequences of insufficient T3. Address the throttle, and many of them self-correct. Attempt to address them individually without restoring the throttle, and you are fighting the current of a system that cannot run at the speed required for self-maintenance.

## How It Works

### Dual Genome Regulation

The mechanism that makes T3 unique among hormones is its simultaneous control of two genomes.

Through nuclear receptors (TR-alpha and TR-beta), T3 [upregulates NRF-1, NRF-2, and mitochondrial transcription factor-A (TFAM)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12552316/), enhancing mitochondrial DNA replication and transcription. This is the well-known pathway: T3 enters the nucleus, binds its receptor, and drives expression of the genes that build mitochondria.

But the c-erbA-alpha gene — the same gene that encodes the nuclear TR-alpha receptor — [simultaneously encodes a mitochondrial T3 receptor called p43](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10567517/). This truncated form localizes to the mitochondrial matrix, where it acts as a [T3-dependent transcription factor of the mitochondrial genome](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18547504/), directly stimulating mitochondrial protein synthesis, respiratory chain activity, and mitochondriogenesis. One gene, two receptors, two genomes — coordinated by a single hormone.

This is why T3 is the throttle, not merely a contributor. Insulin affects glucose uptake. Cortisol mobilizes substrates. Growth hormone promotes anabolism. But none of them directly determine how many respiratory enzyme complexes exist in each cell. T3 does. It sets the ceiling on oxidative capacity itself.

### The Downstream Cascade

Because T3 determines the rate of oxidative metabolism, everything that depends on oxidative metabolism depends on T3. The downstream effects are not secondary or indirect — they are the metabolic consequences of the throttle position.

**Cholesterol conversion and steroidogenesis.** T3 drives the conversion of cholesterol to pregnenolone, the master precursor for all steroid hormones — progesterone, DHEA, cortisol, testosterone, estradiol. The enzyme responsible (CYP11A1) operates inside the mitochondria and requires ATP from oxidative phosphorylation. [Thyroidectomy reduces steroidogenic oxygen consumption by ~30%](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0022473182900310) and impairs both phosphorylative oxidation and energy-dependent hydroxylation. When the throttle drops, cholesterol accumulates in the blood because it is not being converted to protective steroids. This is why elevated cholesterol tracks with hypothyroidism — the substrate is backing up because the enzyme system that consumes it has lost its energy supply. T3 also drives cholesterol conversion to bile acids in the liver, linking thyroid status to fat digestion, intestinal health, and the estrogen-clearing enterohepatic cycle described in [[The Gut-Liver Axis]].

**Progesterone synthesis.** Since T3 drives cholesterol-to-pregnenolone conversion, insufficient T3 directly reduces progesterone production. Less progesterone means less opposition to estrogen. Hypothyroidism produces estrogen dominance through reduced progesterone synthesis — compounded by the pregnenolone steal under chronic stress, which is itself worsened when total pregnenolone production is already low. See [[The Hormonal Axis]].

**Estrogen clearance.** The liver conjugates estrogen for excretion via glucuronidation — an ATP-dependent process. Liver metabolic capacity depends on T3. When thyroid function falls, the liver clears estrogen more slowly, allowing it to accumulate. So hypothyroidism drives estrogen dominance through two simultaneous pathways: reduced progesterone synthesis AND reduced estrogen clearance. The bidirectional amplification is documented in [[The Gut-Liver Axis]]: estrogen, in turn, increases hepatic production of thyroxine-binding globulin, sequestering thyroid hormone in an inactive bound state. Each side worsens the other.

**ATP production and serotonin sequestration.** CO2 and ATP are the co-products of complete oxidative metabolism. When T3 drops, oxidative metabolism slows, both CO2 and ATP production fall. Falling ATP impairs V-ATPase-mediated sequestration of serotonin in platelet and mast cell granules — the mechanism documented in [[The System]]. Serotonin is released. Serotonin then suppresses mitochondrial energy production and causes vasoconstriction, reducing oxygen delivery — which further impairs oxidative metabolism and ATP production. The T3-ATP-serotonin axis (with CO2 as parallel marker) is one of the tightest feedback couplings in [[The System]]. See [[Conservation Signaling]] for the full serotonin mechanism.

**Dopamine and prolactin.** T3 supports dopamine synthesis, and dopamine tonically inhibits prolactin release from the pituitary. When T3 falls, dopamine drops, prolactin rises, and elevated prolactin further suppresses gonadal function. This connects the thyroid throttle directly to reproductive capacity. See [[The Hormonal Axis]].

**Cardiac function.** T3 [upregulates SERCA2a and downregulates phospholamban](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2820695/) — the calcium-handling proteins that enable the heart to relax efficiently between contractions. T3 [reverses ischemia/reperfusion-induced degradation of these proteins](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6880411/) and preserves high-energy phosphates. In cardiac surgery, [T3 deficiency is present in 85% of brain-dead donors](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9086582/), and T3 administration directly [resuscitates failing donor hearts](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8266553/) — [116 consecutive donors treated with T3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2305461/) showed good immediate cardiac function in all but 3 recipients. Cardiac surgeons do not give T4 and wait for conversion. They give T3 because it is the active hormone and the clinical situation requires it immediately.

**Nerve function.** Hypothyroidism produces slowed conduction, slowed recovery, and slowed perception. The Achilles tendon reflex relaxation test measures exactly this: the speed at which a nerve-muscle system restores its energized resting state after stimulation. [Woltman's sign (delayed reflex relaxation)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2466474/) is [92% predictive for hypothyroidism with 100% sensitivity](https://litfl.com/woltman-sign/) — a direct readout of cellular energy status that was used diagnostically before blood tests displaced it.

**Temperature.** Basal body temperature is a direct readout of metabolic heat production, which is a direct readout of mitochondrial oxidative activity. T3 drives this through [activation of mitochondrial uncoupling proteins](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK537039/), thermogenin and the respiratory chain itself. Core body temperature reflects the same process that ETCO2 reflects — the rate at which glucose is being fully oxidized — making it a continuous, freely available indicator of throttle position. Its limitation is confoundability: ambient temperature, hormonal cycles ([progesterone raises temperature 0.3-0.7 degrees C in the luteal phase](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7575238/)), and adrenaline-mediated vasoconstriction can all shift the reading independent of T3. Temperature is sensitive but not specific. In combination with pulse and ETCO2, it becomes far more informative — see [[The Cellular Readout]].

### What Suppresses T3

The same substances that drive the system toward the lactate-dominant attractor independently suppress T3. Each works through a specific, documented mechanism.

**Polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA).** [Unsaturated non-esterified fatty acids inhibit T4 binding to thyroxine-binding globulin and transthyretin](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7488876/), displacing thyroid hormone from its carriers. The effect is [attenuated in vivo by albumin binding](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7488876/) — the in vitro displacement is clear, but physiological magnitude is smaller than laboratory data alone suggests. PUFA also damages mitochondrial membranes directly (see [[Membrane Damage and Defense]]), impairing the respiratory chain that T3 is trying to build. A system loaded with PUFA resists T3's effects even when T3 is present. PUFA suppresses thyroid, thyroid suppression allows estrogen to rise, estrogen increases PUFA retention — a self-deepening loop.

**Estrogen.** Estrogen [inhibits monoamine oxidase-A (MAO-A) by 28% in the hypothalamus](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9698044/), increasing serotonin, which suppresses metabolic rate. Estrogen increases hepatic production of thyroxine-binding globulin, sequestering thyroid hormone in an inactive bound state. And hypothyroidism impairs liver clearance of estrogen — bidirectional amplification. See [[The Hormonal Axis]].

**Cortisol and stress.** Cortisol [inhibits type 1 deiodinase (D1) and shifts T4 conversion toward reverse T3](https://joe.bioscientifica.com/view/journals/joe/205/1/1.xml) instead of T3. Reverse T3 [competitively inhibits DIO1](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/0004563220969150), further impairing T4-to-T3 conversion and creating a self-reinforcing cycle of reduced T3 production. (Note: the older claim that rT3 directly blocks T3 at nuclear thyroid hormone receptors is [not supported by peer-reviewed evidence](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/0004563220969150) — rT3's binding affinity at nuclear receptors is at least 200-fold weaker than T3. The functionally significant mechanism is DIO1 competitive inhibition.) This is the mechanism behind non-thyroidal illness syndrome — the most common pattern in critical illness is low T3, high reverse T3, with normal TSH. Elevated reverse T3 is an [independent predictor of mortality in critically ill patients](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/euthyroid-sick-syndrome). A patient with normal TSH, normal T4, high reverse T3, and low T3 is functionally hypothyroid at the tissue level while appearing euthyroid on standard testing.

**Endotoxin.** [Intravenous LPS in healthy subjects reproduces the full NTIS pattern](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0739724098000320): reduced T3, T4, and TSH with increased reverse T3. LPS [disrupts thyroid cell function through the TLR4-NF-kB pathway](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10884059/) and [alters deiodinase activity](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12171323/). The mechanism is more nuanced than simple direct suppression: LPS [activates type 2 deiodinase (D2) in hypothalamic tanycytes](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16095572/), increasing local T4-to-T3 conversion in the mediobasal hypothalamus — which paradoxically drives TRH suppression and central hypothyroidism. At the whole-organism level, LPS suppresses thyroid function, but via D2 activation producing local hypothalamic T3 excess that shuts down the HPT axis. The relationship is bidirectional: [hypothyroid patients show elevated LPS](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/microbiology/articles/10.3389/fmicb.2025.1661211/full) due to impaired gut motility and barrier integrity, and LPS further suppresses thyroid function — the gut-thyroid-motility circuit detailed in [[The Gut-Liver Axis]].

Each of these suppressors exists in a separate research literature — lipid biochemistry, reproductive endocrinology, stress physiology, gastroenterology. No single discipline integrates across them to ask: what is the combined suppressive load on a person whose diet is high in PUFA, whose estrogen is elevated, whose cortisol is chronically high, and whose gut barrier is compromised? The answer is that these loads are not additive — they are multiplicative, because each one worsens the conditions that activate the others.

This is the architecture that makes hypothyroidism self-sustaining. It is not primarily a gland failure. It is systemic suppression of T3 activity by substances that accumulate precisely because T3 is insufficient to clear them. The throttle drops, the suppressors rise, the suppressors push the throttle further down. Breaking this cycle requires addressing multiple suppressors simultaneously — not merely supplementing the hormone while leaving the suppressive environment intact.

### The Conversion Problem: DIO2

T4 is a prohormone. The thyroid gland secretes [approximately 85 mcg T4 and 6.5 mcg T3 daily](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK537039/) — about 80% of total T3 production comes from [peripheral deiodination of T4 in tissues](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK537039/). This conversion depends on three selenoprotein enzymes: DIO1, DIO2, and DIO3. [All three require selenium in their catalytic sites](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12672807/); [selenium deficiency inhibits T4-to-T3 conversion](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25129137/), producing [high free T4-to-T3 ratios](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7783124/) — the laboratory signature of impaired conversion.

The [DIO2 Thr92Ala polymorphism](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/102/5/1623/2966904), present in [12-30% of the population depending on ethnicity](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11135370/), reduces D2-mediated T4-to-T3 conversion and lowers serum T3 in thyroid-deficient patients. In patients carrying both [DIO2 and MCT10 polymorphisms, 100% preferred combination T4+T3 treatment](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5527224/). The ATA and ETA now [support an individualized trial of T3 addition for symptomatic patients on optimized T4](https://etj.bioscientifica.com/view/journals/etj/10/1/ETJ512970.xml).

This polymorphism resolves an apparent contradiction in the clinical literature. [14 randomized combination therapy trials](https://www.mdpi.com/1422-0067/25/17/9218): 10 showed no superiority of T4+T3, 4 showed some superiority. But [meta-analysis of patient preference](https://www.thyroid.org/patient-thyroid-information/ct-for-patients/february-2025/vol-18-issue-2-p-5-6/): 52% preferred combination, 24% preferred T4 monotherapy, 24% had no preference. The most likely explanation is population heterogeneity: most patients convert T4 adequately and gain nothing from added T3. A genetically identifiable minority does not convert adequately, and for them, T4-only therapy is inherently insufficient regardless of dose. Averaging across both groups produces null results in trials while individual patients experience dramatic improvement. This is a textbook case of a real biological effect being invisible in population-level data because the responders are diluted by the non-responders — and the subgroups can now be identified genetically before treatment begins.

The brain is particularly vulnerable to conversion impairment. [T4 crosses the blood-brain barrier preferentially via OATP1C1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4067591/), then is converted to T3 by D2 in astrocytes. The brain achieves high T3 levels through local enzymatic conversion — making it directly dependent on D2 function. A DIO2 polymorphism carrier taking T4-only therapy may have adequate T3 levels peripherally while being T3-deficient in the brain. In a primate model, [intravenous T3 achieved CSF levels of 3,921 ng/dl vs only 100 ng/dl for T4](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6822088/), demonstrating that T3 can penetrate when directly provided.

The mineral dependency chain extends further: [zinc is essential for thyroid peroxidase (TPO) expression](https://econtent.hogrefe.com/doi/10.1024/0300-9831/a000262) via thyroid transcription factor 2, for thyroglobulin synthesis, and as a [cofactor for deiodinases](https://econtent.hogrefe.com/doi/10.1024/0300-9831/a000262) alongside selenium. [Zinc deficiency produces subclinical hypothyroidism](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11432327/) and is associated with higher prevalence of TPO antibodies. The thyroid system requires iodine (substrate), selenium (deiodinase catalytic site), and zinc (TPO expression and deiodinase cofactor) — three minerals whose adequacy must be simultaneous. Deficiency in any one produces bottleneck effects.

### The Diagnostic Problem

The standard diagnostic test for thyroid function is TSH — thyroid stimulating hormone, a pituitary signal. When the pituitary senses low thyroid hormone, it releases more TSH to drive the gland harder. High TSH is interpreted as hypothyroidism; normal TSH is interpreted as euthyroidism.

The problem is that TSH can be suppressed by factors that have nothing to do with adequate thyroid hormone in tissues.

**Non-thyroidal illness syndrome (NTIS).** In critical illness, [T3 drops, reverse T3 rises, and TSH appears normal or low](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK285570/). The mechanism: [cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-alpha) directly suppress TSH](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK482219/) while [cortisol inhibits T4-to-T3 conversion](https://joe.bioscientifica.com/view/journals/joe/205/1/1.xml). In acute hospital settings, [~3% of patients show TSH <0.1 mU/L](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK285570/), and in 75% of these, the low TSH results from NTIS or medications, not hyperthyroidism. A sick patient with low T3 and normal TSH is functionally hypothyroid by every physiological measure while appearing euthyroid on the standard test.

**The reference range.** The NHANES III-derived recommendation to [narrow the upper TSH limit to 2.5 mU/L](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1089/thy.2005.15.1035) has not been adopted by most labs, which retain the 0.5-4.5/5.0 range. The debate is [explicitly acknowledged as unresolved in Nature Reviews Endocrinology](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrendo.2011.13). A TSH of 0.5 and a TSH of 4.5 are both "normal" despite a 9-fold difference. TSH also shifts higher with age, further complicating interpretation.

**Bidirectional noise — the overtreatment paradox.** A [Yale study estimated 21 million of 23 million Americans taking levothyroxine may not need it](https://medicine.yale.edu/news-article/21-million-americans-may-take-a-hypothyroidism-drug-they-dont-need/) due to seasonal TSH variation causing unnecessary diagnosis. TSH fluctuates with seasons, circadian rhythm, and acute stress — a single snapshot above the threshold triggers a diagnosis and a prescription that may persist for years.

Simultaneously, [10-25% of treated patients have persistent symptoms despite normal TSH](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/cen.14473). In a UK study, [40.3% of treated patients had hypothyroid symptoms vs 24.9% in controls](https://www.endocrine.org/news-and-advocacy/news-room/2018/patients-on-levothyroxine-continue-to-experience-symptoms-despite-normal-thyroid-hormone-levels). These patients have been told their thyroid is "treated" because TSH normalized — yet they remain functionally hypothyroid by every clinical measure. The diagnostic loop closes: TSH normalizes, therefore the treatment is working, therefore the remaining symptoms must have another cause.

The same tool simultaneously overdiagnoses some people (seasonal TSH elevations treated unnecessarily) and underdiagnoses others (functionally hypothyroid patients with stress-suppressed TSH told they're normal, or treated patients whose T4 isn't converting). This is not one error in one direction — it is noise in both directions from a proxy variable. No other major diagnostic system in medicine produces this pattern of simultaneous mass overtreatment and systematic undertreatment from the same test.

**TSH has direct pro-inflammatory effects.** TSH itself [increases mast cell populations and triggers degranulation](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6449760/) and [induces IL-6 expression in fibrocytes](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0075100). Elevated TSH is not metabolically neutral — it is a signal with its own tissue effects.

**Functional metabolic measurements capture what TSH misses.** [End-tidal CO2 levels are lower in both subclinical and overt hypothyroidism](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3056328/) than in healthy controls, with only a weak statistical correlation to TSH (r=-0.24, p=0.01) and none to fT4. A physiological marker reflects a metabolic state that the standard blood test does not. The mechanism is more nuanced than direct CO2 production: ETCO2 reflects the alveolar gas equilibrium between CO2 production and ventilation, distinct from V'CO2 (the direct CO2 production rate measured by indirect calorimetry). The framework's broader prediction — that hypothyroidism reduces oxidative metabolism — is independently established: [BMR falls ~40% in overt hypothyroidism](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK500006/), and [a 2021 case used indirect calorimetry to detect undiagnosed hypothyroidism](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33689191/) through V'CO2-derived REE 39% below predicted. Whether the lower ETCO2 in subclinical/mild hypothyroidism reflects reduced production, chronic hyperventilation as part of the disease attractor (see [[The Metabolic Fork]]), or both, has not been directly measured. The directional finding holds; the mechanism is unresolved. See [[The Cellular Readout]] for the full monitoring framework.

The diagnostic system measures a pituitary signal (TSH) and a prohormone (T4) while explicitly excluding measurement of the active hormone (T3) and the functional outputs of thyroid action. Current guidelines [state that serum T3 should not be used to diagnose hypothyroidism](https://www.endocrinepractice.org/article/S1530-891X(20)43030-7/fulltext). This means the system that identifies hypothyroidism does not measure the hormone whose deficiency defines the condition. Imagine diagnosing diabetes without measuring blood glucose — only measuring the pancreatic signal that's supposed to produce it. That is the structural analogy to TSH-only thyroid diagnosis.

## Where the Evidence Is Complex

> [!warning] TSH Is Noisy in Both Directions
> TSH-based diagnosis simultaneously produces massive overtreatment ([21 million potentially unnecessary prescriptions](https://medicine.yale.edu/news-article/21-million-americans-may-take-a-hypothyroidism-drug-they-dont-need/) from seasonal TSH variation) and systematic undertreatment ([10-25% of treated patients with persistent symptoms](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/cen.14473), plus an unknown number of functionally hypothyroid people with stress-suppressed TSH). The assumption that this test is simply too sensitive or too insensitive is wrong — it is unreliable in both directions, depending on context.

> [!warning] Historical Prevalence Estimates Are Unvalidated
> The estimate that hypothyroidism affects 30-40% of the population, based on basal temperature measurements from the mid-20th century, has [never been validated by modern epidemiological methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Broda_Otto_Barnes). Current prevalence estimates (~5% clinical, 3-15% subclinical) use different criteria. The historical shift from BMR-based to TSH-based diagnosis did redefine the disease, but whether the pre-TSH estimates were more accurate or simply less specific is genuinely unknown.

> [!warning] Combination Therapy Trials Are Mixed
> [14 randomized trials](https://www.mdpi.com/1422-0067/25/17/9218) of T4+T3 combination therapy: 10 showed no superiority over T4 alone for quality-of-life endpoints. The likely explanation — population heterogeneity where DIO2 carriers benefit while normal converters do not — is plausible and increasingly supported, but remains a hypothesis being tested, not a settled finding.

> [!warning] FDA Action Against Desiccated Thyroid
> The [FDA announced enforcement action against unapproved animal-derived thyroid medications in August 2025](https://www.medscape.com/viewarticle/fda-warns-future-action-animal-derived-thyroid-drugs-2025a1000lqn), potentially removing desiccated thyroid products from the market. Desiccated thyroid accounts for [~6% of thyroid medication users](https://www.medfinder.com/blog/armour-thyroid-shortage-what-providers-prescribers-need-to-know-2026) (1.5M vs 22M patients). This may narrow therapeutic options at the moment the evidence base is widening to support combination therapy for a genetically identifiable subpopulation.

> [!warning] ETCO2 Findings Are Real but the Mechanism Is Contested
> [ETCO2 is lower in hypothyroidism](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3056328/) — a striking single finding (Ansarin et al. 2011, 95 Iranian women) that has not been replicated. The strict claim "no correlation with TSH" reflects the original authors' framing; the actual reported correlation is r=-0.24, p=0.01 — statistically significant but clinically negligible (~6% variance explained). More importantly, the original authors interpret the lower ETCO2 as **hyperventilation** (increased minute ventilation blowing off CO2), not reduced CO2 production. The mechanism is unresolved without direct V'CO2 measurement, though both interpretations are consistent with the framework: reduced production via the throttle being lowered, AND chronic hyperventilation as part of the disease attractor (see [[The Metabolic Fork]] on chronic hyperventilation depleting CO2). The broader claim that hypothyroidism reduces oxidative metabolism is independently established by [BMR data](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK500006/) and [indirect calorimetry case findings](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33689191/) — measurements that capture V'CO2 directly rather than ETCO2's production-ventilation balance. ETCO2 remains useful as an individual trend marker. Its formal diagnostic sensitivity and specificity against established thyroid tests have not been validated.

## Research Context

Levothyroxine (synthetic T4) is the [2nd-4th most prescribed drug in the United States](https://www.goodrx.com/synthroid/why-synthroid-is-the-most-prescribed-drug-in-the-us) with [102.6 million prescriptions in 2019](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/2781311). The global levothyroxine market is valued at [$4.4-4.6 billion](https://www.businessresearchinsights.com/market-reports/levothyroxine-market-121143), led by AbbVie's Synthroid brand, with [North America accounting for ~45.5% of global revenue](https://www.researchnester.com/reports/levothyroxine-sodium-market/6956).

The diagnostic infrastructure and therapeutic infrastructure are mutually reinforcing. TSH is the primary test. T4 normalizes TSH. Therefore T4 is the treatment. Therefore TSH is the test. This closed loop does not test whether tissues received adequate T3. When a patient on levothyroxine reports persistent fatigue, brain fog, and weight gain with a normal TSH, the standard response is that the thyroid is "treated" — the remaining symptoms must be something else. The possibility that T4 is not converting to T3 adequately is outside the diagnostic loop.

There is no commercial sponsor for large trials of desiccated thyroid or T3 combination therapy — the molecules are generic or under regulatory threat. The vast majority of thyroid treatment research uses levothyroxine as the intervention and TSH as the primary endpoint. No trial has measured the timescale of tissue-level metabolic recovery — the restoration of mitochondrial respiratory enzyme expression, normalization of membrane composition, and recovery from the downstream cascade (progesterone synthesis, estrogen clearance, serotonin suppression) — because the diagnostic framework does not consider these endpoints.

The disciplinary fragmentation is stark:

- **Endocrinology** diagnoses by TSH, treats with T4, explicitly excludes T3 from the standard workup
- **Cardiac surgery** administers T3 directly to [brain-dead donors](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9086582/) to resuscitate failing hearts — treats T3 as the critical active hormone
- **Critical care** recognizes non-thyroidal illness syndrome where TSH is misleading during illness
- **Psychiatry** studies T3 augmentation in treatment-resistant depression
- **Genetics** studies DIO2 polymorphisms affecting T4-to-T3 conversion

Cardiac surgeons use T3 to save organs while endocrinologists debate whether to prescribe it. Critical care physicians recognize that TSH is unreliable during illness while primary care uses it as the sole screening test. Psychiatrists augment antidepressants with T3 while the endocrine guidelines state T3 should not be used to diagnose hypothyroidism. The research literature contains the evidence for T3's importance — distributed across specialties that do not read each other's journals. The fragmentation is not just between specialties — it is between what different specialties consider the same molecule to be worth.

## Practical Implications

> [!tip] For the full intervention and monitoring framework, see [[Practice]].

- **T3 status determines the ceiling on metabolic recovery.** Addressing PUFA, estrogen, or gut health without adequate T3 removes suppressors from a system that still cannot run. T3 sets the rate at which every corrective process operates.
- **DIO2 polymorphism testing identifies who needs T3 directly.** [12-30% of the population](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11135370/) carries Thr92Ala. For these patients, T4-only therapy may be inherently insufficient regardless of dose optimization. Genetic testing before initiating thyroid therapy could prevent years of treatment with a prohormone that cannot be adequately converted.
- **Physiological monitoring complements blood testing.** Temperature + pulse + ETCO2 provide real-time functional readouts that detect metabolic state. ETCO2 is the most direct single measure of oxidative metabolism. These should be used alongside (not instead of) TSH/fT4/fT3 to identify the mismatch between biochemical normalization and functional status that affects [1 in 4 treated patients](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10686697/).
- **Address the suppressors, not just the hormone.** PUFA, estrogen, cortisol, and endotoxin each independently suppress T3. Supplementing thyroid hormone without addressing these suppressors forces an ongoing pharmacological battle against the metabolic environment. This is why effective intervention targets multiple inputs simultaneously — see [[Practice]].
- **Mineral cofactors must be simultaneous.** Iodine (substrate), selenium (deiodinase catalytic site), and zinc (TPO expression and deiodinase cofactor) — deficiency in any one creates a bottleneck that thyroid hormone supplementation cannot overcome.
