# Ray Peat — Historical Assessment

**What this document is:** A first-principles assessment of Ray Peat's accuracy as a biological thinker, based on the systematic verification, extraction, and stress testing documented in this project. Not a biography or tribute — an honest accounting of what he got right, what he got wrong, and the consistent pattern that explains both.

**Traceability:** Every assessment here traces to specific project work:
- `project_history/peat_claims_verified/` — the claim-by-claim verification against peer-reviewed research (16 topic documents)
- `metabolic_framework/` — the unified vault: extracted standalone biology with all stress test refinements incorporated (14 documents, 221 citations audited)
- `project_history/metabolic_refinements/` — the stress test audit trail (3 layers tested, 6 refinement documents)

## The Overall Picture

Peat saw the whole map correctly but drew some of the roads wrong. His accuracy increases as you move from molecular detail toward the big picture — the things that matter most, he got most right; the things he got most wrong matter least, because molecular details are correctable without changing the architecture they plug into.

## What He Got Right

### The thesis — the deepest claim

Health IS efficient oxidative metabolism. Disease IS its progressive failure. This is not "metabolism is one factor among many." Metabolism is health at the cellular level. Every function a cell performs requires ATP. When ATP production is adequate, functions work. When it fails, they don't. What we call "disease" is what cellular energy failure looks like from different angles, in different tissues, at different stages.

This identification — confirmed at structural, mathematical, cellular, and whole-organism levels by the stress test — is Peat's most fundamental contribution. No mainstream medical framework makes this claim. The evidence for it existed in the peer-reviewed literature across multiple specialties. Peat assembled it.

### The system architecture

The metabolic system can exhibit two self-reinforcing stable states — maintained by interlocking positive feedback loops — when parameter conditions (PUFA burden, iron load, chronic stress, endotoxin exposure) create a disease basin. Health maintains health through its own outputs. Disease produces more disease through its own outputs. Crossing from one state to the other requires addressing multiple inputs simultaneously because the loops compensate for single interventions. Peat overclaimed this as unconditional (see "What He Got Wrong" below), but the architecture itself is confirmed.

This is confirmed as genuine dynamical-systems bistability — not metaphor. The stress test validated it at four independent levels: structurally (10+ positive circuits satisfying Thomas's necessary condition), mathematically (Topp et al. saddle-node bifurcation), cellularly (Mulukutla et al. glycolysis/OXPHOS bistability with experimental hysteresis), and at the whole-organism level (bariatric surgery duration-dependent remission as hysteresis, Tabak et al. longitudinal critical slowing down).

### The key variables

Peat correctly identified the variables that matter most:

- **Thyroid (T3)** as the master throttle — independently validated by resmetirom (FDA-approved 2024, THR-beta agonist for NASH)
- **PUFA** as the primary hardware damage substrate — independently validated by the ferroptosis field (Stockwell 2012)
- **Estrogen** as the glycolytic shift driver, opposed by **progesterone**
- **Endotoxin** as the gateway stressor that activates every disease mediator simultaneously
- **Serotonin** as a conservation/stress signal, not a "happiness molecule" — one of his most contrarian positions, substantially vindicated by the Moncrieff 2022 umbrella review and the SSRI-allopregnanolone finding
- **The liver** as the metabolic hub where every pathway converges
- **Light/dark** as the environmental input driving daily oscillation between attractors
- **Lactate** as an active disease driver — independently validated by the histone lactylation discovery (Zhang et al. 2019, *Nature*)

### The integration

This is what Peat got MOST right — not any single mechanism but the seeing-it-as-one-system. He traced connections from mitochondrial respiration through thyroid signaling, PUFA peroxidation, estrogen dynamics, gut-liver interactions, serotonin physiology, and circadian biology into a single coherent picture. No single researcher in any mainstream specialty had done this. The evidence was scattered across endocrinology, hepatology, oncology, neurology, cell biology, and immunology. The integration itself is the contribution that no discipline had made.

### The practical direction

All 8 core interventions survived verification:
1. Reduce PUFA tissue burden
2. Support thyroid function
3. Maximize light, minimize darkness stress
4. Favor glucose oxidation (with PDH cofactors)
5. Support gut integrity (motility via thyroid, not probiotics)
6. Oppose estrogen with progesterone
7. Reduce chronic stress (metabolic and psychological converge on HPA axis)
8. Balance amino acid intake (glycine/gelatin alongside muscle meat)

The leverage ordering is approximately correct. The practical direction is right even where the specific mechanism explanation is wrong.

## What He Got Wrong

### CO₂ as a causal driver

The single biggest error. Peat treated CO₂ as an active agent sustaining oxidative metabolism through feedback loops — CO₂ maintaining pH to sequester mediators, CO₂ supporting thyroid function. The stress test found that ATP, not CO₂, is the causal agent at every feedback loop target. CO₂ is stoichiometrically co-produced with ATP at OXPHOS but has no specific molecular mechanisms at any loop target — only non-specific bulk pH effects, while ATP has specific mechanisms everywhere (V-ATPase catalytic site, tight junction maintenance, smooth muscle contraction).

CO₂'s non-loop roles are real — the Bohr effect for oxygen delivery, carbamino bonds for protein protection. But these are not feedback loop drivers. After the stress test, CO₂ appears in zero formalized loops as a causal variable. Every instance where Peat says "CO₂ does X" should be read as "ATP does X, and CO₂ is the parallel marker you can monitor."

Note: CO₂'s non-loop claims held up remarkably well in verification. Of 8 CO₂ claims tested, 6 were confirmed outright (including the Bohr effect, ROS suppression, RQ framework, and hyperventilation pathology at 87-100% seizure provocation), 1 confirmed with nuance (carbamino protection — chemistry confirmed proteome-wide, aldehyde protection hypothesis untested), and 1 speculative (methylation/epigenetic claim). The CO₂ error is architectural — where it sits in the feedback loops — not substantive. CO₂ is a genuinely active molecule with specific molecular mechanisms at targets Peat never discussed (soluble adenylyl cyclase bicarbonate binding, connexin 26 hemichannel gating via K125 carbamylation, ubiquitin K48 carbamylation suppressing NF-κB — all structurally characterized, causally confirmed with mutant evidence). It is not a causal driver in the specific feedback loops Peat attributed to it.

**Why CO₂, specifically.** The attribution isn't arbitrary — it follows from Peat's coacervate ontology for mitochondria. In his framework, mitochondria are phase-separated structures whose stability depends on interfacial chemistry at the phase boundary. The natural regulatory agents in such a framework are small, diffusible molecules that alter interfacial forces — exactly what CO₂ is. Replace the coacervate ontology with the endosymbiont/membrane-bound-organelle picture (which requires active ATP-dependent transport and protein import for organelle maintenance), and ATP becomes the natural regulatory agent. The CO₂→ATP correction is therefore a consequence of correcting his ontology, not an independent molecular error. See "The physical-chemistry preference pattern" section below.

### The six contradictions

Out of 118+ claims verified, six were outright contradicted or not supported in the original verification project (a seventh has been identified through subsequent article study work):

1. **Lactate brain excitotoxicity** — directly contradicted. Acute lactate is neuroprotective, reduces glutamate release (Mazuel et al., *J Neuroscience*). The sharpest reversal in the vault.
2. **Methylglyoxal from lactate** — direction reversed. MGO comes from glycolytic intermediates and is detoxified *into* D-lactate, not the reverse.
3. **SSRI non-selectivity** — not pharmacologically supported. SSRIs are genuinely selective for SERT. The SSRI critique holds on other grounds (placebo proximity, allopregnanolone mechanism, German BGA rejection) — just not this one.
4. **Brain serotonin increases with aging** — not confirmed by PET/SPECT imaging. Receptors and transporters actually decline with age.
5. **Membrane pump rejection** — SERCA/PMCA are real, now with atomic-resolution crystal structures (2025 Nature). Peat's bulk-water-structure alternative is wrong. But the downstream prediction (calcium exclusion is energy-dependent) is correct — the mechanism IS the pumps, and the pumps require ATP.
6. **Barnes 30-40% hypothyroid prevalence** — unsupported by any modern validation. Current estimates: ~5% clinical, 3-15% subclinical.
7. **Endosymbiont theory rejection** — the mainstream theory that mitochondria originated as bacteria entering into endosymbiosis with an ancestral eukaryote. Peat preferred a coacervate origin — mitochondria as phase-separated structures arising through proteinoid microsphere dynamics. The endosymbiont evidence is overwhelming and independent: circular bacterial-type mtDNA, 70S ribosomes, bacterial-type cardiolipin in the inner membrane, protein import machinery (TIM/TOM) traceable to bacterial homologs, and phylogenomic placement firmly within the alpha-proteobacteria. Multiple methods converge. This rejection pairs with #5 (membrane pump rejection) as the clearest instances of the physical-chemistry preference pattern overriding well-established molecular biology. See "The physical-chemistry preference pattern" section below.

That half of these contradictions still have correct downstream predictions despite wrong mechanisms is itself diagnostic of the "right destination, wrong route" pattern.

### Specific mechanism pathways — the "right destination, wrong route" pattern

This pattern recurs across nearly every topic. The gap between ~80% directional accuracy and ~50-60% pathway accuracy IS this pattern — quantified:

| Claim | Direction | Mechanism Peat proposed | Actual mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| Estrogen shifts cells to glycolysis | Correct | Direct Complex IV blockade | PI3K/AKT transcriptional reprogramming |
| Stress depletes progesterone | Correct | Pregnenolone steal (substrate competition) | HPA axis suppression of GnRH/LH |
| CO₂ sequesters mediators | Correct direction | CO₂ → carbonic acid → granule pH | ATP → V-ATPase → granule pH |
| Lactate drives disease | Correct (chronic) | Lactate as metabolic waste | Histone lactylation as epigenetic reprogramming |
| Serotonin suppresses metabolism | Correct | Direct mitochondrial inhibition | Conservation signaling program |
| Progesterone stabilizes cells | Correct | Direct membrane stabilization | Allopregnanolone → GABA-A receptor modulation |
| Progesterone anti-inflammatory | Correct | Direct enzyme inhibition | Progesterone-induced proteins (PIBF, uteroglobin) |
| Brain concentrates T3 from serum | Correct destination | Active T3 concentration from serum at 6x | T4 crosses BBB via OATP1C1, local D2 conversion in astrocytes |

The pattern makes sense at two levels. Historically, Peat was working with biochemistry available through the 1990s-2000s. The molecular tools that revealed the actual mechanisms — Seahorse Bioanalyzer, cryo-EM, iPSC-derived cell models, mass spectrometry proteomics — didn't exist when he formulated most of his positions.

But the deeper reason is structural: Peat was reasoning backward from correct system-level observations to mechanisms. When you see the right outcome and ask "what mechanism could produce this?", you naturally pick the most parsimonious explanation — the most direct causal chain available. CO₂ → carbonic acid → granule pH is more parsimonious than ATP → V-ATPase → granule pH → V₁-V₀ sensor. Pregnenolone steal (substrate competition) is more parsimonious than HPA axis suppression of GnRH/LH. The more direct explanation was consistently wrong, but the reasoning strategy — working backward from correct observations — is what *produced* the consistent "right destination, wrong route" pattern. The strategy validates itself even as the specific attributions need correction: if the destinations are reliably right, the observer is seeing something real about the system.

### Unconditional claims that are actually conditional

Peat's consistent error was flattening genuinely dual-identity molecules into single poles. This appeared across every topic:

- **Bistability is unconditional** → it's conditional on parameter values. Some organisms are monostable — the healthy state is the only attractor and single interventions work fine.
- **Lactate is harmful** → chronic lactate is harmful (drives epigenetic reprogramming via lactylation). Acute lactate is neuroprotective and a normal exercise metabolite.
- **Melatonin is harmful** → melatonin drives the conservation program (correct), but is also anti-estrogenic at the tissue level (SERM, 50% aromatase inhibition), rejuvenates the aged thymus, and has no in vivo pro-oxidant activity. Three of Peat's four sub-claims about melatonin's harm are contradicted at the tissue level.
- **Aspirin is protective** → protective in younger populations with intact immune surveillance. ASPREE showed increased cancer mortality in healthy elderly — the most extreme context-dependence in the framework.
- **Iron is uniformly toxic** → iron accumulation drives disease (correct), but acute/chronic and dose-dependence matter. Teriparatide (intermittent PTH) builds bone; chronic PTH destroys it. The same acute/chronic distinction Peat applied to some molecules he failed to apply to others.
- **PUFA effects on mast cells** → peroxidation products destabilize mast cells (correct for chronic peroxidized PUFA), but intact PUFAs can stabilize mast cells acutely through different receptors — another acute/chronic distinction he missed.

### The physical-chemistry preference pattern

Beneath the individual errors is a single methodological preference that generates them: Peat consistently preferred physical-chemistry explanations over specific molecular machinery. When biology offered two candidate explanations — one grounded in general physical principles (phase dynamics, interfacial forces, bulk water structure) and one grounded in specific molecular mechanisms (specific pumps, specific organelles with specific genomes, specific protein complexes) — he consistently chose the physical-chemistry version.

The preference was explicit. In the 1990s article "Mitochondria and Mortality" he rejects endosymbiont theory as intellectually lazy, writing that it "can stop people from thinking freshly about the nature of the various organelles, and how they came to exist." The objection is methodological, not empirical. He acknowledged the theory existed and had support; he rejected it because he thought specific molecular machinery was a just-so story that closed off deeper inquiry.

The preference is philosophically defensible as an instinct. Biology does ground in physical chemistry at the deepest level — living systems are dissipative structures, and Prigogine, Schrödinger, and Szent-Györgyi were right that there is a thermodynamic floor below specific molecular mechanisms. The floor exists. The error was collapsing the floor with the causal actor above it. The fact that a living system is maintained against entropy by continuous energy throughput (true) does not mean specific molecular machinery is not doing the specific work (also true). The thermodynamic framework is the *context* within which the molecular actors operate; it is not itself the actor. Peat collapsed the two, and the collapse produced his rejection pattern.

**The two clearest instances.**

**Endosymbiont rejection.** Mainstream cell biology has accepted since the 1980s, on overwhelming independent evidence (circular bacterial-type mtDNA, 70S ribosomes, cardiolipin, bacterial protein import machinery, phylogenomic placement in alpha-proteobacteria), that mitochondria are descended from bacteria that entered into endosymbiosis with an ancestral eukaryotic host. Peat rejected this in favor of a coacervate origin — mitochondria as phase-separated structures arising through proteinoid microsphere dynamics. The coacervate framework is general physical chemistry; the endosymbiont framework is specific molecular biology. He chose the former over the latter despite the evidence being well-established when he was writing.

**Membrane pump rejection.** Standard cell biology attributes cellular calcium exclusion to SERCA, PMCA, and NCX — all ATP-dependent pumps maintaining the 10,000:1 gradient through active transport. Peat preferred explanations rooted in bulk water structure and the cytoplasm's general physical-chemical properties (drawing on Gilbert Ling's association-induction hypothesis). The pumps are specific molecular machinery; the water-structure framework is general physical chemistry. He chose the latter. SERCA and PMCA now have atomic-resolution crystal structures confirming the pump mechanism.

**Why these cases differ from the CO₂→ATP error.** The CO₂ attribution has a real methodological excuse: the molecular tools that characterized V-ATPase at specific loop targets were largely developed after Peat's framework was established. Given the tools available, his attribution was reasonable at the time. The endosymbiont and pump rejections don't have that excuse — the molecular biology was mainstream consensus when he was writing, and he rejected it on explicitly methodological grounds.

**Why these errors are related, not independent.** The coacervate ontology and the bulk-water framework are the same kind of intellectual move — both privilege general physical-chemical principles over specific molecular machinery. The CO₂→ATP error inherits directly from the coacervate ontology: in a coacervate framework, CO₂ is the natural small-molecule phase stabilizer. These are not separate mistakes but a single methodological preference expressing itself at multiple levels. Correcting the ontology substantially corrects the currency attribution that follows from it.

**Implication for reading Peat.** When his specified mechanism conflicts with well-established molecular biology, the conflict is almost always diagnostic of this framework preference overriding the evidence. The system-level observation is often still correct; the mechanism he cites is usually the wrong one precisely because he chose physical-chemistry framework over molecular machinery. This is the cleanest predictor of where his errors cluster.

### Missing: state-dependent damping

The deepest structural insight the stress test produced was something Peat never articulated: every self-correcting mechanism the body has requires ATP to function. The positive feedback loops that maintain disease operate BY weakening the energy-dependent negative feedback. This is multiplicative, not additive — the effective gain of the positive feedback increases as the negative feedback it degrades falls away. This is the mechanism that PRODUCES bistability, and Peat described the bistability without identifying what generates it.

### Citation-level errors

The verification and stress test found that Peat's specific citations frequently don't support the claims he deploys them for. The pattern: citations that are topically relevant but mismatched on species (animal data applied as if human), scenario (in vitro results applied as if in vivo), endpoint (proxy measures treated as direct evidence), or mechanism (the paper attributes the finding to a different mechanism than Peat claims). This is the "bulk-effect attribution" pattern — claims stated at a level of generality that doesn't match the citation's specific context.

## How to Read Peat Today

### Read him as a map-maker, not a molecular biologist

His value is in the connections he drew between domains — the integration that no specialist had assembled. When he traces a line from thyroid to liver to estrogen to gut to serotonin to PUFA and back, the connections are real even when the specific mechanisms linking them need updating. The map is right. Some of the roads need redrawing.

### When he says "this causes that" — trust the this and that, verify the causes

The variables he identifies are usually correct. The causal mechanism connecting them is right about half the time at the molecular level. The clinical direction is reliable. The biochemical pathway he attributes it to may be the most direct explanation available to him rather than the actual one.

### When he talks about CO₂ — read it as ATP

Every time. The functional picture he describes is real — metabolic output sustaining the conditions for more metabolism. But the variable doing the causal work is ATP, not CO₂. CO₂ is the readout you can monitor (via ETCO₂). ATP is the agent doing what he says CO₂ is doing.

### When he dismisses something categorically — add conditionality

Lactate is harmful → chronic lactate (acute lactate is neuroprotective). Serotonin is harmful → as a tonically elevated conservation signal (acute neuronal serotonin may enhance mitochondrial function). Melatonin is harmful → as a driver of the nightly conservation dip (but anti-estrogenic at the tissue level, rejuvenates aged thymus — three of four harm sub-claims contradicted). Estrogen is harmful → when the system is in the disease attractor (protective in metabolically healthy cells). Aspirin is protective → in younger populations with intact immune surveillance (ASPREE showed increased cancer mortality in elderly). His descriptions are accurate for the chronic disease state. They overclaim when applied universally or to controlled biological deployments.

### When he talks about the system having two states — add parameter conditions

Peat described two attractors as an unconditional fact about the metabolic system. The stress test confirmed the bistability is real but conditional — it depends on parameter values (PUFA tissue burden, iron load, chronic stress, endotoxin exposure) falling within the bistable region. An organism with low damage burden and robust energy-dependent negative feedback may be monostable — the healthy state is the only attractor. When Peat describes the two-attractor architecture, the dynamics are correct for organisms whose parameters have created a disease basin. They are not universal.

### When he says something is universally true — add regime

He didn't have the bistability framework formalized with its conditional structure. Not everyone needs the full multi-loop intervention approach. Context-dependence and metabolic regime matter. His recommendations are strongest as descriptions of what the bistable disease state requires, not as universal biochemical truths applicable to every organism in every state.

### When he cites evidence — verify independently

Citation deployment errors cluster where mechanisms are named at the bulk-effect level. His citations often topically match but don't specifically support the deployment he uses them for. This doesn't mean the claim is wrong — citation accuracy and claim accuracy are separate questions, and the claim is often supported by evidence he didn't cite. But the specific paper he points to may not say what he says it says.

### When he connects things no one else connects — that's where he's most valuable

The moments where he integrates across disciplines that don't cite each other — that's the contribution no one else made. The connections are confirmed by the evidence. The integration itself is what mainstream medicine is missing, and it's what Peat saw that almost no one else did.

### Know where his errors actually cluster — the three-tier pattern

His reliability varies systematically by level of claim.

**System-level observations — usually right.** When Peat describes what the *system* does — lactate as causal rather than marker, serotonin as conservation signal, PUFA as hardware damage, estrogen driving glycolysis, thyroid as master throttle, light activating respiration — trust him as a first pass. System-level observations don't require getting the mechanism right. He was reading the integrated organism directly, and reading it well. Most of the ~40% confirmed-outright claims sit here.

**Mechanism attributions where molecular machinery wasn't yet characterized — right direction, wrong route.** When Peat specified a mechanism in a domain where the specific molecular biology hadn't been worked out, he typically got the direction right and the specific mechanism wrong. The CO₂→ATP error, the pregnenolone-steal mechanism, estrogen's direct Complex IV blockade, progesterone's direct membrane stabilization — all fit this pattern. Read his mechanism claims here as approximately right in direction but likely to need replacement at the specific pathway level. Most of the ~39% confirmed-with-nuance claims sit here.

**Mechanism attributions where molecular machinery was well-characterized but conflicted with his framework — systematically wrong.** When Peat rejected mainstream molecular biology in favor of a general physical-chemistry explanation, he was almost always wrong on the mechanism. The membrane pump rejection and the endosymbiont rejection are the clearest examples. Both cases involve well-established biology available and accepted when he was writing; he chose his framework over the evidence on explicitly methodological grounds. When you encounter a Peat mechanism claim that contradicts standard cell biology, this is the fingerprint. The system-level observation still often holds; his specified mechanism is almost certainly the wrong one.

**The practical rule.** Trust Peat confidently at the system level. Hold his mechanism claims cautiously when the underlying molecular biology wasn't yet characterized in his time. Be actively skeptical when his mechanism claim contradicts well-established molecular biology that predates his writing — that conflict is almost always diagnostic of framework preference overriding evidence, and the specific molecular biology is almost always right.

## The Historical Context

Peat was working from approximately the 1970s through the 2010s, building his integration primarily from mid-20th century biochemistry (Hans Selye's stress physiology, Otto Warburg's cancer metabolism, Albert Szent-Gyorgyi's bioenergetics, Broda Barnes' clinical thyroid work) combined with his own reading of the primary literature across multiple disciplines.

The molecular revolution that would have refined his mechanism attributions — ferroptosis (2012), histone lactylation (2019), single-cell metabolomics, cryo-EM structural biology, iPSC-derived disease models — largely occurred after his framework was established. The "right destination, wrong route" pattern is exactly what you'd expect from someone who saw the system-level truth correctly but was working before the tools existed to trace the molecular paths precisely.

His work was never published in peer-reviewed journals and exists primarily as articles on his website and in his self-published newsletters. This means it was never subjected to formal peer review — which explains both its freedom to integrate across disciplinary boundaries (no reviewer from a single specialty could evaluate the whole picture) and its vulnerability to mechanism-level errors (no specialist checked the specific molecular claims). The verification project documented in this workspace is, in a sense, the peer review his work never received.

## What Survives and What Doesn't

**Survives completely (~40% of claims — confirmed outright):** The thesis. The integration. The identification of health with oxidative metabolism. The two-attractor architecture. The key variables. The practical direction of all 8 interventions. The emphasis on thyroid as rate-setter, PUFA as damage substrate, estrogen/progesterone balance, gut-liver axis as gateway, light as environmental driver. The Fenton reaction chain. The Randle cycle as mechanism-in-common. The Bohr effect elevated to framework principle. Hyperventilation pathology. The endotoxin-barrier vicious circle. Glycogen-glucuronidation dependence. EFA-deficient endotoxin resistance. PUFA requirement for alcoholic liver injury. Tissue estrogen accumulation (5-50x serum). The historical PUFA consumption shift.

**Survives with refinement (~39% of claims — direction right, mechanism or scope needs qualification):** The bistability claim (conditional, not unconditional). The substrate specificity (Krebs-cycle substrates, not glucose exclusively). The loop architecture (10 clean, not 13). The acute/chronic distinction (needs formal control-architecture criteria, not just timescale). Estrogen as glycolytic driver (PI3K/AKT, not Complex IV). Progesterone as protector (via allopregnanolone/GABA, not direct membrane stabilization). Serotonin as conservation mediator (with receptor-specific neuronal effects preserved). CO₂ as protective metabolite (confirmed) but not as causal loop driver (ATP is). Sugar as preferred substrate (with glucolipotoxicity caveat and PDH cofactor requirements). Melatonin as conservation driver (with anti-estrogenic and thymus-rejuvenating properties preserved).

**Does not survive (~4% of claims — contradicted):** CO₂ as a causal driver in feedback loops. Pregnenolone steal mechanism. Lactate brain excitotoxicity (acute lactate is neuroprotective). Methylglyoxal-from-lactate direction. SSRI non-selectivity. Brain serotonin increase with aging. Membrane pump rejection (SERCA/PMCA are real). Coacervate origin of mitochondria (endosymbiont theory holds — bacterial-type mtDNA, 70S ribosomes, cardiolipin, phylogenomic placement in alpha-proteobacteria). Barnes 30-40% hypothyroid prevalence. Unconditional harmfulness of melatonin at tissue level. The universal applicability of the multi-loop intervention approach (some people are monostable).

**Was missing entirely:** State-dependent damping — the deepest mechanism producing bistability. The formal bistability analysis with its six testable predictions. The causal-independence audit methodology. The regime assessment that determines intervention strategy. The histone lactylation mechanism (discovered 2019) that made lactate's disease-driving role stronger than Peat described. The PDH cofactor vulnerabilities (B1, Mg) that gate his glucose-first recommendation.

The framework built from Peat's starting point is now more precise, more predictive, more falsifiable, and more honest than his original work. The distance traveled is significant: 118+ claims verified across 16 topics, ~79% confirmed at the directional level, ~4% contradicted, the rest refined. Peat saw two states; the project proved bistability with four independent confirmations and identified the conditions under which it emerges. Peat saw interconnection; the project mapped 10 clean feedback loops with a formal causal-independence audit grounded in Pearl's screening-off test and metabolic control analysis. Peat saw that disease maintains itself; the project identified state-dependent damping as the mechanism — the deepest structural insight, which Peat never articulated. Peat described the system; the project generated six testable predictions from it, two already observed in existing clinical data.

The gap between ~80% directional accuracy and ~50-60% pathway accuracy is the quantitative signature of a thinker who saw the system correctly and explained the mechanisms with the best tools available to him. The correcting instruments — Seahorse Bioanalyzer, cryo-EM, iPSC-derived cell models, mass spectrometry proteomics, the ferroptosis field, the lactylation discovery — largely arrived after his framework was established. The destinations were reliably right. The routes need redrawing. That pattern, across 118 claims, is not coincidence — it is what happens when someone sees something real about a system before the molecular resolution exists to trace it precisely.

The project went from intuition to formalism, from integration to dynamics, from "this is how it works" to "here is what it predicts and here is what would falsify it." Every correction made the framework stronger. That is exactly what the work was meant to do — not build a monument but find what's most true.
