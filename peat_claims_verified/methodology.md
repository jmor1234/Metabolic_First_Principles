# Methodology: Verifying Peat's Claims Against Modern Research

## What This Is

This document describes the methodology used to produce every document in this directory. It exists so that future work on additional topics follows the same process and maintains the same rigor. Any agent continuing this project should read this document first.

The goal is not to summarize Peat's work. Summaries exist elsewhere. The goal is to build a systematic bridge between Peat's cross-disciplinary integration and the fragmented mainstream literature that independently confirms, refines, or contradicts it. Each document extracts Peat's full reasoning from his primary sources, understands his first-principles logic as he actually articulated it, then tests each claim against modern peer-reviewed research while accounting for the incentive structures, measurement choices, and disciplinary fragmentation that shape what that research can and cannot see.

---

## The Three-Phase Process

### Phase 1: Extract and Understand Peat's Actual Position

**This phase must be completed fully before any verification begins.**

The most common failure mode is jumping to research verification before understanding what Peat actually said, why he said it, and how his reasoning chain connects to the broader framework. Surface-level summaries of his claims produce surface-level verification. The extraction must go deep enough to reconstruct his reasoning chain from first principles.

**How to do it:**

1. **Identify and fetch Peat's primary source articles** from raypeat.com. Most topics have 2-5 dedicated articles. Search `site:raypeat.com [topic]` to find them. Fetch the full content, not summaries.

2. **Extract not just claims but reasoning chains.** Peat didn't make isolated assertions. He traced mechanisms across domains. For each claim, document: what he said happens, through what specific mechanism, connecting to what other systems, and why it matters within the broader metabolic framework. The reasoning chain is as important as the conclusion.

3. **Identify how the topic connects to every other topic in the series.** Peat's framework is a single integrated system. Each topic feeds into and receives from other topics. PUFA connects to thyroid, estrogen, serotonin, and CO2 through specific mechanisms. Thyroid connects to progesterone, cholesterol, serotonin, and lactate. These connections are not optional context. They are the framework.

4. **Organize claims into distinct categories.** Each topic typically contains 5-8 distinct claim categories. Some are mechanistic (how X does Y). Some are diagnostic (how to measure Z). Some are historical (how the understanding shifted). Some are practical (what to do about it). Organizing them clearly makes phase 2 tractable.

5. **Document Peat's actual sources.** List the specific raypeat.com articles each claim draws from, with URLs. This allows anyone to go back to the primary material.

6. **Write the first-principles logic for each claim.** After documenting what Peat said and the mechanism he described, add a section explaining how this claim follows from the framework's foundational premises. This is what separates extraction from mere quotation. The logic section shows why the claim is not arbitrary but follows from the thermodynamic and biochemical principles the framework is built on.

7. **Stop.** Do not proceed to verification until the user has reviewed the extraction and confirmed it captures the full context. Premature verification against an incomplete understanding of the original position produces shallow analysis.

### Phase 2: Identify the Research Context Before Searching

**Before searching for research, explicitly identify the contextual factors that shape the research landscape for this topic.**

Every topic has a different distortion pattern. Some have pharmaceutical interests actively opposing Peat's position (serotonin/SSRIs, thyroid/Synthroid). Some have industry momentum promoting a narrative that contradicts him (PUFA/seed oil industry, lactate/sports performance). Some have institutional neglect rather than active opposition (CO2, progesterone). Some have diagnostic infrastructure that encodes assumptions Peat challenged (thyroid/TSH testing).

**What to identify before searching:**

1. **Who funds the research on this topic?** Industry-funded studies have structural incentives to validate the products they sell. This doesn't make them wrong, but it shapes which questions get asked, which comparisons get made, and which conclusions survive peer review.

2. **What comparison is the research making?** Many studies compare two options within a paradigm Peat rejected entirely. Fish oil vs. seed oil tells you about relative PUFA toxicity, not about whether PUFA is good. SSRIs vs. placebo tells you whether the drug has effects, not whether serotonin elevation is therapeutic. The comparison determines the conclusion. If the comparison doesn't match the question Peat's framework asks, the study cannot answer it regardless of its quality.

3. **What timescale does the research cover?** The acute/chronic distinction proved essential across serotonin, CO2, and lactate. Acute effects can differ from or even oppose chronic effects. Exercise lactate (acute, transient, useful) differs from chronic disease lactate (sustained, pathological). Acute neuronal serotonin signaling (receptor-specific, mitochondria-enhancing) differs from chronic systemic serotonin elevation (metabolically suppressive). If a study measures the acute state and Peat's claim concerns the chronic state, the study cannot refute or confirm the claim.

4. **What endpoint does the research measure?** Studies measuring proxy markers (TSH, CRP, blood lipids) may diverge from studies measuring functional outcomes (temperature, tissue oxygenation, mitochondrial membrane composition, actual mortality). Fish oil improves plasma lipids while worsening mitochondrial oxidative stress. SSRIs lower inflammatory markers while increasing suicide risk. The endpoint determines the conclusion. If the measured endpoint is not the thing that actually matters, the conclusion may be misleading.

5. **What is the institutional and disciplinary fragmentation?** Peat's claims typically integrate findings from 3-5 disciplines that don't reference each other. Identify which disciplines hold relevant evidence and search across all of them, not just the dominant one. The gut physiologist studying serotonin, the cardiologist studying carcinoid valve disease, the gerontologist studying tryptophan restriction, and the psychiatrist studying SSRIs all have pieces of the serotonin picture. No single discipline has the whole picture.

### Phase 3: Verify Against Research With Full Context

**Search for and evaluate research on each claim category, reading through the contextual factors identified in the pre-search analysis.**

**How to do it:**

1. **Search for the mechanism, not just the conclusion.** Look for studies that tested the specific biological mechanism Peat described, not just studies that reached the same or different conclusions from different methods. A study confirming cardiolipin remodeling with aging is more relevant than an epidemiological study on fat intake and mortality, because it tests the specific mechanistic link in the chain.

2. **Read the data, not just the abstract.** A paper whose results section shows increased lipid peroxidation but whose conclusion says "omega-3 supplementation was beneficial based on primary endpoint X" contains evidence for Peat's position in its own data while reaching a different conclusion. The data and the interpretation can diverge, especially when the interpretation is shaped by the prevailing paradigm or funding source.

3. **Distinguish between what a study shows and what it claims to show.** A study "showing no benefit of T3 addition" may have enrolled all hypothyroid patients regardless of conversion status, diluting the signal from poor converters with non-responders. A study "showing fish oil reduces inflammation" may be measuring immunosuppression and calling it anti-inflammation. The framing of the finding is a claim about the finding, not the finding itself.

4. **Use multiple search angles for each claim.** Search the mechanism directly, search for contradicting evidence, search across the relevant disciplines identified in the pre-search analysis. Don't stop at the first confirming or disconfirming result. The goal is a complete picture, not confirmation.

5. **Assess honestly.** The methodology requires distinguishing between:
   - **Confirmed** — mainstream research supports the claim through published, peer-reviewed evidence
   - **Confirmed with nuance** — the direction is right but the mechanism is more complex, more upstream, or more context-dependent than Peat stated
   - **Partially supported** — some evidence supports the claim but other evidence complicates or limits it
   - **Overstated** — the direction may be right but the absolute version of the claim goes further than evidence supports
   - **Speculative** — the theoretical logic is sound but direct experimental evidence is insufficient
   - **Not supported** — the weight of evidence runs against the claim

   Forcing every claim into "confirmed" is as dishonest as dismissing every claim as "unproven." The methodology requires calibration, not advocacy.

6. **Note what's missing.** Some of Peat's most interesting claims correspond to experiments that have never been conducted. The carbamino bond protein protection hypothesis. The chronic subclinical endotoxin effect on outpatient metabolism. The CO2-demethylase activation proposal. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence when the experiment hasn't been run. Flag these as untested predictions rather than unconfirmed claims.

7. **Write contextual commentary.** After presenting the evidence for each claim, add commentary that integrates the finding with the broader analysis. How does this confirmation or refinement connect to the other topics? Does it reveal a pattern (like the acute/chronic distinction) that applies across the framework? Does it expose a measurement gap or incentive distortion in the research landscape? The commentary is where the analysis becomes more than a literature review.

## Principles

**Understand before verifying.** Phase 1 must be complete before phase 2 begins. An incomplete understanding of Peat's actual position produces strawman verification.

**Context before content.** The research landscape's incentive structure, comparison choices, timescales, and endpoint selections shape what the literature can see. Identifying these before searching prevents naive reading of research conclusions.

**Read across disciplines.** Peat's claims integrate findings from multiple fields. Verification must search across the same fields. A claim about serotonin may require evidence from gastroenterology, psychiatry, cardiology, gerontology, and exercise physiology.

**The acute/chronic distinction applies everywhere.** Most biological signals are beneficial when acute and pathological when chronic. If a study measures the acute state and Peat's claim concerns the chronic state, they are studying different phenomena. Both findings can be simultaneously valid.

**Honest assessment is non-negotiable.** The goal is not to defend Peat. It is to determine what the evidence actually shows when read through the full context. Some claims will be confirmed. Some will be refined. Some will be overstated. Some will lack evidence. The methodology requires reporting all of these accurately.

**Practical implications must follow from the analysis.** Each document should close with what the verified claims mean for someone applying the framework. The practical section is not appended — it flows from the analysis.

**Connect everything.** Each document exists within a system where every topic connects to every other topic. The connections are not optional framing. They are the framework's core contribution. A document that verifies claims in isolation without showing how they feed into and receive from the other topics has missed the point.

## Recurring Patterns Discovered During Verification

These patterns emerged from the work itself. Future agents should watch for them because they recur across topics and inform how to interpret evidence correctly.

### "Right Destination, Slightly Wrong Route"

Three times across the series, Peat identified the correct functional outcome but attributed it to the most direct mechanism available with his data, when the actual mechanism was more upstream or indirect:

- **Estrogen**: correct endpoint (glycolytic shift), attributed to direct Complex IV blockade, actual mechanism is AKT/ERR signaling cascades operating at physiological concentrations
- **Progesterone/cortisol**: correct endpoint (anti-stress), attributed to substrate competition, actual mechanism is allopregnanolone/GABA/HPA axis modulation
- **Serotonin/mitochondria**: correct concern (metabolic suppression), stated as blanket inhibition, actual effects are cell-type and dose-dependent

In each case, the correcting studies used instruments (Seahorse Bioanalyzer, cryo-EM, iPSC-derived cell models) that didn't exist when Peat formulated his positions. When this pattern appears, it means Peat made the most logical inference from available data and subsequent technology refined the mechanism without overturning the conclusion. Flag it as a refinement, not a refutation.

### The Acute/Chronic Distinction

Most biological signals are beneficial when pulsatile and pathological when chronic. This emerged independently in serotonin (acute neuronal mitochondrial enhancement vs chronic systemic metabolic suppression), CO2 (brainstem chemosensory activation vs peripheral serotonin suppression), lactate (acute exercise fuel shuttle vs chronic disease marker), and estrogen (acute neuroprotection vs chronic neurotoxicity). When mainstream research contradicts Peat by showing a substance has "beneficial effects," check whether the study measured the acute or chronic state. Peat's claims concern the chronic state. The acute findings are typically real but describe different physiology.

### Context-Dependence

Estrogen revealed that the same substance at the same dose can have opposite effects depending on the metabolic state of the organism. Estrogen amplifies the existing metabolic trajectory: beneficial in a healthy, well-oxidizing context, pathological in a compromised, glycolytic context. This principle may apply to other substances in the framework. When evidence appears genuinely mixed (some studies show benefit, some show harm), investigate whether the difference correlates with the metabolic context of the study population rather than assuming one set of studies is simply wrong.

### Chemical Conflation in the Literature

Research on "progesterone" frequently studies synthetic progestins (medroxyprogesterone acetate, norethindrone) — patentable molecules with different biological properties than bioidentical progesterone. The results are attributed to "progesterone" in both clinical guidelines and popular reporting. This creates a systematic misrepresentation: findings from a synthetic analogue are treated as evidence about the natural hormone. Watch for this with any substance that exists in both natural and pharmaceutical forms (thyroid: desiccated vs synthetic T4; estrogen: estradiol vs conjugated equine estrogens; fatty acids: natural vs modified forms).

### The Diagnostic Measurement Divergence

TSH doesn't reliably indicate tissue thyroid status. Blood estrogen doesn't indicate tissue estrogen concentration. The measured variable diverges from the biologically relevant variable under exactly the conditions where accuracy matters most (stress, aging, chronic illness). When Peat challenges a diagnostic standard, the pattern to look for is: does the measured proxy diverge from tissue-level reality under the conditions the framework identifies as pathological? If so, the diagnostic system will systematically miss the population most affected.

### Fragmentation as the Default Finding

At every topic, the mainstream literature contained the evidence for Peat's integrated picture in fragmented form across specialties that don't reference each other. This is not a finding about any single topic. It is a structural feature of how biomedical research is organized. The integration itself is the discovery. Future documents should explicitly identify which disciplines hold relevant evidence and search across all of them.
