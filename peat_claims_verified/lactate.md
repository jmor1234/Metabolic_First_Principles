# Lactate: The Marker and Mechanism of Metabolic Failure

## Overview

If [[co2|CO2]] is the output of successful oxidative metabolism, lactic acid is the output of its failure. They sit on opposite sides of the same metabolic fork: pyruvate either enters the mitochondria for complete oxidation to [[co2|CO2]], or it is converted to lactate in the cytoplasm. The CO2/lactate ratio is the most direct measurement of whether a tissue is oxidizing glucose efficiently or diverting it to glycolysis.

Peat's lactate claim goes further than "lactate indicates metabolic failure." He argued lactate is a direct cause of further damage: it promotes inflammation, excitotoxicity, fibrosis, edema, and the metabolic conditions that produce more lactate. The stressed organism produces lactate, and lactate deepens the stress.

This claim puts Peat in direct opposition to a recent trend in mainstream metabolism research. The post-2000 "lactate shuttle" literature (George Brooks and others) has rehabilitated lactate, reframing it as a useful fuel that is shuttled between tissues, consumed by the heart and brain, and functioning as a signaling molecule. Peat argued this reframing obscures lactate's pathological role, confusing the body's ability to metabolize lactate with evidence that lactate is beneficial.

## Claim 1: Lactate Is Not Just a Marker but a Direct Cause of Tissue Damage

### Peat's Position

Peat catalogued specific mechanisms through which lactate actively worsens the conditions that produce it. Every mechanism creates a feedback loop that deepens the metabolic failure producing the lactate in the first place.

> [!quote] Peat on excitotoxicity
> "Lactate directly contributes to the damage, for example in the brain, causing nerve cell loss by increasing the release of excitotoxic glutamate."

Lactate doesn't merely correlate with brain damage — it drives the glutamate cascade that kills neurons. This connects directly to [[serotonin]]: [[serotonin]] promotes glycolysis and lactate production, and the lactate then promotes the excitotoxic damage that serotonin-driven vasoconstriction initiated.

> [!quote] Peat on inflammation
> "Lactate and inflammation promote each other in a vicious cycle."

Lactate causes macrophages and synovial fibroblasts to release PGE2 (prostaglandin E2), which promotes inflammation and bone resorption. PGE2 in turn activates [[estrogen]] formation, feeding back into the estrogen-glycolysis pathway.

**Fibrosis.** Lactate stimulates collagen synthesis even in oxygen-sufficient conditions. It promotes the inappropriate connective tissue deposition that characterizes aging organs — the same fibrotic process that [[serotonin]] drives through 5-HT2A/2B receptors. Lactate and serotonin converge on fibrosis through different but complementary pathways.

**Edema.** Lactate causes vasodilation and edema — the swollen, water-logged cellular state that reflects energy failure. This is the opposite of the structured, low-water state maintained by adequate [[co2|CO2]]-producing oxidative metabolism.

**Blood rheology.** Lactate increases red cell aggregation ("blood sludge"), rigidity, and blood viscosity. It displaces [[co2|CO2]] from blood, affecting clotting, and contributes to disseminated intravascular coagulation. This impairs microcirculation, reducing oxygen delivery, which reduces oxidative metabolism, which produces more lactate.

> [!quote] Peat on immune suppression
> "The lactate in peritoneal dialysis solution impairs differentiation and maturation of dendritic cells, with important implications for the initiation of immune responses under high lactate conditions."

Lactate suppresses the immune system's ability to mount effective responses — the same immunosuppressive direction that [[pufa|PUFA]], [[serotonin]], and [[estrogen]] all drive.

**Metabolic self-inhibition.** Lactate slows its own glycolytic production through feedback inhibition but simultaneously suppresses respiratory activity. The net result is reduced total ATP production from both pathways. The cell produces less energy by every route — the energy crisis the framework identifies as the core of disease.

Together, these pathways explain why metabolic decline, once it crosses a threshold, becomes self-sustaining and progressive.

### Verification

> [!success] Confirmed — Mechanism More Powerful Than Peat Could Have Known
> Histone lactylation (discovered 2019-2026) confirms lactate doesn't just acidify tissue — it rewrites macrophage gene expression to make them pro-fibrotic.

**Lactate promotes fibrosis through histone lactylation.** A [2021 study in the American Journal of Respiratory Cell and Molecular Biology](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7780997/) demonstrated that lung myofibroblasts promote macrophage profibrotic activity through lactate-induced histone lactylation. Lactate drives p300-mediated histone lactylation in macrophage promoters, enhancing pro-fibrotic gene expression. A [2026 study in Nature Communications](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-69388-y) confirmed macrophage-derived lactate drives fibroblast phenotypic remodeling in hypertrophic scar via histone H3 lysine 23 lactylation. A [comprehensive 2025 review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12211883/) describes lactylation as a "novel perspective on fibrosis pathogenesis" across organ systems.

The mechanism Peat described (lactate stimulates collagen synthesis and fibrosis) has been confirmed at the epigenetic level through a pathway that wasn't even known when he wrote about it. The mechanism is more specific and more powerful than he could have known.

**Lactate and brain excitotoxicity — both directions documented.** [Lactate contributes to excitotoxic neuronal loss during ischemia](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14980811/) through acidosis-mediated mechanisms, supporting Peat's claim. However, [L-lactate can also protect neurons against glutamate excitotoxicity](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep21250) through ATP-mediated signaling, and [lactate-mediated neuroprotection requires the mitochondrial aspartate-glutamate carrier ARALAR](https://www.jneurosci.org/content/36/16/4443).

This is the acute/chronic distinction playing out exactly as predicted. Acute, controlled lactate delivery to neurons with functional mitochondria can serve as fuel and be neuroprotective. Chronic lactate elevation in ischemic tissue with dysfunctional mitochondria drives excitotoxic damage. Peat was describing the chronic pathological state. The neuroprotection studies describe the acute physiological state.

**Lactate as clinical mortality predictor — firmly established.** In critical care, [elevated blood lactate is one of the strongest predictors of mortality](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379417/). Categorization into normal (<2 mmol/L), mild (2-4 mmol/L), and severe (>4 mmol/L) provides "essential prognostic information." [Normalization of lactate within 6 hours is associated with lower mortality](https://ccforum.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s13054-016-1403-5). [Cumulative lactate exposure correlates with hospital mortality](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3599274/) across heterogeneous ICU populations.

The clinical literature treats lactate elevation as unambiguously harmful in the context of illness — Peat's context. The fact that the exercise physiology literature simultaneously treats acute exercise lactate as a useful fuel shuttle does not contradict the clinical finding. These are different phenomena studied in different populations under different conditions.

## Claim 2: The Four Named Effects as a Continuum of Respiratory Failure

### Peat's Position

Peat placed four well-known metabolic phenomena on a single continuum, with lactate as the common output:

**The Pasteur effect** — the normal, healthy state. In the presence of oxygen, cells restrain glycolysis and fully oxidize glucose in mitochondria. Oxygen inhibits lactate production. This is the definition of efficient oxidative metabolism.

**The Crabtree effect** — a pathological response. Excess glucose suppresses mitochondrial respiration and promotes calcium uptake. Even in the presence of oxygen, high glucose drives glycolysis. This occurs in cancer cells and rapidly proliferating normal cells.

**The Randle effect** — the fat-glucose competition. Free fatty acids inhibit glucose oxidation, shifting metabolism toward protein and fat consumption. This is the mechanism through which [[pufa|PUFA]] and stress hormones (which mobilize fatty acids) suppress the glucose oxidation that would produce [[co2|CO2]]. The shift produces less [[co2|CO2]] and more lactate.

**The Warburg effect** — the endpoint. Cells produce lactate despite adequate oxygen. Aerobic glycolysis. This is the defining metabolic feature of cancer, and Peat argued it is also the endpoint of chronic respiratory failure in any tissue: aging, chronic disease, and neurodegeneration all involve the progressive establishment of the Warburg state.

> [!quote] Peat's integration
> These are "points on a continuum of mitochondrial dysfunction."

The Pasteur effect is health. The Warburg effect is cancer and terminal metabolic failure. The Crabtree and Randle effects are intermediate stages. The progression from Pasteur to Warburg is the progression from health to disease, traced through specific, named metabolic transitions, with lactate as the common marker of each stage's failure.

### Verification

> [!info] Integration Is Peat's — Not Mainstream
> Each effect is individually recognized in biochemistry. No mainstream publication frames all four as stages of a single progressive respiratory failure.

The Pasteur, Crabtree, Randle, and Warburg effects are all individually recognized. The Warburg effect is oncology. The Randle cycle is diabetes and metabolic syndrome. The Crabtree effect is yeast biochemistry and occasional cancer metabolism. The Pasteur effect is textbook introductory biochemistry.

Peat's contribution was placing them on a single continuum of progressive respiratory failure. No mainstream publication we found frames all four as stages of a single process with lactate as the common output. The integration remains Peat's.

## Claim 3: Cancer as the Endpoint of Chronic Respiratory Failure

### Peat's Position

This is where Peat's framework intersects most directly with Otto Warburg's original research. Warburg demonstrated in the 1920s that "a respiratory defect, causing aerobic glycolysis, was an essential feature of cancer."

Peat argued that modern oncology acknowledged Warburg's finding while fitting it into the "genetic mutant cell paradigm" — treating the Warburg effect as a consequence of oncogene mutations rather than investigating the causes of the respiratory defect itself. The mainstream asks: which mutations cause the glycolytic shift? Peat's framework asks: what environmental and metabolic conditions cause the respiratory failure that precedes the mutations?

The answer within the framework: [[pufa|PUFA]]-damaged mitochondrial membranes, [[thyroid#Claim 1|thyroid insufficiency]] reducing respiratory enzyme expression, [[estrogen]] signaling cells toward glycolysis, [[serotonin]] inhibiting respiration, and endotoxin directly suppressing mitochondrial function. Each topic in this series describes a pathway to the Warburg state. Cancer is the terminal expression of metabolic dysfunction in a tissue pushed past the point where oxidative metabolism can be maintained.

> [!quote] Peat on lactate as cancer promoter
> "Several specific signals produced by lactate can promote growth: HIF, NF-kappaB, the kinase cascades, cyclin D1, and heme oxygenase."

Lactate doesn't just accompany cancer — it activates the signaling pathways that drive cancer growth. The tumor's glycolytic metabolism creates the biochemical environment that sustains and expands the tumor.

**Fatty acid synthase.** In cancer cells, FAS is highly active and its activity inversely predicts prognosis. FAS is activated by cortisol and [[estrogen]]. Peat described it as functioning as an "electron sink" when respiration fails. Aspirin inhibits FAS, which partially explains aspirin's documented anticancer properties.

> [!quote] Peat on starvation and tumors
> "Starving an animal with a tumor increases the stress hormones, providing free fatty acids and amino acids, and accelerates the tumor's growth."

The tumor's independence from glucose oxidation means caloric restriction feeds the stress-metabolism axis (cortisol, [[pufa|PUFA]] mobilization, Randle effect) without starving the cancer.

### Verification

> [!tip] Confirmed With Nuance — Warburg Is Back, But Framed Differently
> Aerobic glycolysis is now a recognized hallmark of cancer. Whether respiratory failure causes or follows oncogene mutations remains debated.

**The Warburg effect is now a recognized hallmark of cancer.** [Aerobic glycolysis is acknowledged as a defining feature of cancer metabolism](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4783224/). This is no longer controversial. What remains debated is causation.

**Warburg's original claim (respiratory defect causes cancer) is partially rehabilitated but not dominant.** The [2021 Journal of Physiology review](https://physoc.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1113/JP278810) "revisiting the Warburg effect" notes that researchers in the 1950s-70s demonstrated most tumors were not accompanied by complete mitochondrial dysfunction. The current mainstream position is that oncogene mutations (Ras, Myc, PI3K) drive the glycolytic shift through growth factor signaling and HIF-1 stabilization, with mitochondrial dysfunction playing a secondary role in a minority of tumors.

However, this framing has a blind spot. The question "which mutations cause the glycolytic shift?" assumes the glycolytic shift is genetically initiated. Peat's question was different: what environmental and metabolic conditions cause the respiratory failure that precedes the mutations? If [[pufa|PUFA]]-damaged cardiolipin, [[thyroid#Claim 1|thyroid insufficiency]], [[estrogen]]-driven glycolytic signaling, and endotoxin-mediated mitochondrial suppression progressively impair respiration in a tissue, the Warburg state may be established before any oncogene mutation occurs. The mainstream studies this question rarely because the experimental design assumes genetic initiation.

**Lactate activates cancer growth signaling — confirmed.** Lactate [activates HIF-1alpha through PKM2 interaction](https://www.nature.com/articles/ncomms5436) in macrophages, driving aerobic glycolysis and HMGB1 release during sepsis. The same HIF-mediated glycolytic activation occurs in tumors. Lactate is not just a byproduct of cancer metabolism — it is an active participant in maintaining the cancer phenotype.

## Claim 4: The Lactate/CO2 Ratio as Master Diagnostic

### Peat's Position

Peat identified the [[co2|CO2]]/lactate ratio as the most fundamental readout of metabolic health. This connects the lactate document to [[co2]] and [[thyroid#Claim 1|thyroid's]] diagnostic claims.

> [!quote] Peat on lactate and thyroid
> "When T3 is metabolically active, lactic acid won't be produced unnecessarily, so the measurement of lactate in the blood is a useful test for interpreting thyroid function."

> [!quote] Peat on CO2 measurement
> "A measurement of the percentage of carbon dioxide at the end of a single breath can give an indication of the stress-free, thyroid hormone stimulated rate of metabolism (it should approach five or six percent of the expired air)."

The two measurements together — end-tidal [[co2|CO2]] and blood lactate — capture the metabolic state more completely than any other combination of tests. High [[co2|CO2]] and low lactate means glucose is being fully oxidized, [[thyroid]] is adequate, respiratory enzymes are functional, and the organism is in the healthy metabolic state. Low [[co2|CO2]] and high lactate means glucose is being diverted to glycolysis, mitochondria are underperforming, and the organism is in the stressed, degenerating state.

This is the measurable, objective version of what temperature and pulse approximate: the ratio of oxidative to glycolytic metabolism in real time.

### Verification

> [!success] Confirmed — Brain Lactate as Aging Hallmark
> A PNAS study demonstrated twofold brain lactate increase in aging mice driven by LDH-A/LDH-B ratio shift. LDH overexpression shortens lifespan; downregulation extends it.

**High brain lactate is a hallmark of aging.** A [PNAS study](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1008189107) demonstrated that brain lactate levels increase twofold in aging mice, driven by a shift in the LDH-A/LDH-B ratio. The increased LDH-A (which converts pyruvate to lactate) relative to LDH-B (which converts lactate to pyruvate) is causative. The researchers proposed lactate levels "could predict aging."

In Drosophila, [LDH overexpression in both neuronal and glial cell types caused significant reduction in lifespan, whereas LDH downregulation resulted in lifespan extension](https://www.aging-us.com/article/103373/text). This is a direct experimental test of whether lactate production drives aging: forcing cells to make more lactate shortens life, forcing them to make less extends it. This confirms lactate as a cause, not just a marker, of aging.

Cerebrospinal fluid lactate is elevated in aging humans and in Alzheimer's disease patients. [Elevated concentrations of lactate reduce glucose utilization](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7955001/) in cultured astrocytes, neurons, and brain tissue in a concentration-dependent manner, confirming the metabolic self-inhibition Peat described: lactate suppresses the glucose oxidation that would prevent its own formation.

## Claim 5: Convergence — Every Topic Feeds Into Lactate

### Peat's Position

Lactate is the common pathway where every mechanism documented in this series converges.

**[[pufa|PUFA]]** damages mitochondrial membranes through cardiolipin peroxidation, reducing respiratory capacity and diverting pyruvate to lactate. See [[pufa#Claim 1]].

**[[estrogen|Estrogen]]** reprograms cells toward glycolysis through AKT signaling, LDH upregulation, and TCA cycle suppression. See [[estrogen#Claim 1]].

**[[serotonin|Serotonin]]** directly activates phosphofructokinase (the rate-limiting glycolytic enzyme), increases plasma lactate, and inhibits mitochondrial respiration — causing "dramatic fall in brain ATP level alongside increased anaerobic glycolysis and elevated lactate." See [[serotonin#Claim 1]].

**[[thyroid#Claim 1|Hypothyroidism]]** reduces expression of respiratory enzymes (the master throttle), reducing the cell's capacity to oxidize glucose fully. See [[thyroid]].

**Cortisol** mobilizes free fatty acids, triggering the Randle effect. The shift to fat oxidation reduces [[co2|CO2]] production and increases lactate.

**Endotoxin** directly inhibits mitochondria and can produce aerobic glycolysis in an otherwise healthy person (Bundgaard et al., 2003).

**Stress hormones** create the metabolic cascade: hypoglycemia from accelerated glycolysis stimulates cortisol, cortisol increases protein breakdown and fat mobilization, free fatty acids inhibit glucose oxidation (Randle), insufficient [[co2|CO2]] prevents ammonia-to-urea conversion, ammonia stimulates further lactate formation.

This convergence explains why the framework's intervention strategy addresses multiple inputs simultaneously rather than targeting any single pathway.

### Verification

> [!success] Confirmed — Endotoxin Produces Aerobic Glycolysis in Healthy People
> A single dose of bacterial endotoxin shifts healthy human metabolism from oxidative to glycolytic.

[Endotoxin administration to healthy volunteers](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12446281/) induced lactate production from skeletal muscle under fully aerobic conditions. Plasma lactate increased from 0.9 to 2.5 mmol/L with no associated hypoperfusion or hypoxia. This is aerobic glycolysis — the Warburg effect — induced in healthy tissue by an environmental insult, not by genetic mutation or cancer.

The mechanism involves catecholamine-driven Na+/K+-ATPase activation in skeletal muscle. But the broader significance is what Peat emphasized: the Warburg state is not exclusive to cancer. Endotoxin, which enters systemic circulation from the gut daily in varying amounts depending on gut barrier integrity, represents a chronic low-level stimulus toward aerobic glycolysis. The difference between overt sepsis and chronic subclinical endotoxin exposure is magnitude and timescale, not mechanism.

[Lactate also directly causes immunosuppression in sepsis](https://journals.lww.com/shockjournal/FullText/2018/02000/Lactate_and_Immunosuppression_in_Sepsis.2.aspx), connecting to the [[pufa]] immunosuppression finding. The immune system is suppressed by the metabolic state it's trying to fight.

## Claim 6: Lactate in Specific Clinical Conditions

### Peat's Position

Peat connected lactate to specific conditions not as a shared biomarker but as a shared mechanism — the respiratory defect producing the glycolytic shift producing lactate producing further damage.

**Cancer.** Aerobic glycolysis (Warburg effect). Lactate activates HIF, NF-kappaB, and growth signaling. See Claim 3 above.

**Heart failure.** Cardiac lactate reflects impaired myocardial oxidation. [[thyroid#Claim 1|T3]] regulates calcium-binding proteins essential for cardiac relaxation; hypothyroid hearts fail to relax properly, accumulate water, and shift toward glycolytic metabolism.

**Brain injury and neurodegeneration.** Lactate increases excitotoxic glutamate release, causes nerve cell loss, increases intracellular fat accumulation, chromatin clumping, and mitochondrial swelling. [[serotonin]]-driven vasoconstriction reduces brain oxygenation, forcing the shift to glycolysis.

**Shock.** Lactate is the established clinical marker of inadequate tissue perfusion. But lactated Ringer's solution, the standard resuscitation fluid, itself "contributes to tissue damage in shock" (Deree et al., 2007-2008). See the Ringer's note under Practical below.

**Diabetes.** Methylglyoxal, formed from lactate, causes protein glycation. This connects to the [[pufa]] lipofuscin finding: "advanced glycation end products" attributed to sugar are more likely driven by lactate-derived methylglyoxal and [[pufa|PUFA]]-derived aldehydes.

**Wound healing.** Acute lactate stimulates collagen synthesis and angiogenesis (adaptive for tissue closure). Chronic elevation drives pathological fibrosis, converting the healing response into a degenerative one.

**Aging.** Lactate accumulation participates in protein glycation, collagen abnormalities, tissue matrix degradation, and mitochondrial dysfunction. Correcting the respiratory defect would be relevant for all conditions involving inflammation and inappropriate excitation.

### Verification

> [!tip] Confirmed With Nuance — Ringer's Solution
> Bicarbonate Ringer's outperforms lactate Ringer's for inflammation; lactate Ringer's still outperforms normal saline.

[A randomized controlled trial comparing bicarbonate-based Ringer's to lactate-based Ringer's in hemorrhagic shock](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9678593/) found that bicarbonate Ringer's inhibited inflammatory factor expression and complications were significantly lower (15.15% vs 40.0%). However, [lactated Ringer's outperformed normal saline](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39969246/) in sepsis-induced hypotension (12.2% vs 15.9% mortality).

The honest reading: bicarbonate-based solutions appear superior to lactate-based for inflammatory suppression, supporting Peat's concern. But lactated Ringer's is still better than normal saline, because saline causes its own problems (hyperchloremic acidosis). The clinical reality is more nuanced than "lactated Ringer's is harmful."

[Methylglyoxal and AGEs in diabetes](https://journals.physiology.org/doi/full/10.1152/physrev.00001.2019) — Physiological Reviews, 2020 — confirms methylglyoxal forms from glycolytic intermediates, not glucose directly, supporting Peat's reattribution of glycation damage from glucose to lactate-derived metabolites.

## The Lactate Shuttle Question — Addressed Honestly

The [astrocyte-neuron lactate shuttle (ANLS)](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnins.2017.00043/full) hypothesis represents the strongest mainstream argument that lactate is beneficial: astrocytes produce lactate and shuttle it to neurons as fuel.

However, the ANLS is [scientifically contested within the mainstream itself](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/jnr.24015). Critics argue it lacks appropriate stoichiometric support. [Neurons are fully capable of glucose uptake and glycolysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S036192301730638X) without astrocyte-derived lactate. Simulations supporting the ANLS require increasing astrocytic glucose transport capacity 12-fold and assuming neurons don't respond to activation with increased glycolysis — "conditions that are not supported by current literature."

Even accepting the ANLS as partially valid, it describes acute, low-concentration fuel shuttling between cell types under normal physiological conditions. It does not address chronic systemic lactate elevation, which the [PNAS aging study](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1008189107) confirmed as a hallmark of brain aging driven by LDH isoform shifts, and which the [PMC study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7955001/) confirmed reduces glucose utilization in a concentration-dependent manner. The shuttle hypothesis and the chronic elevation pathology describe different phenomena at different concentrations and timescales.

## What Makes This Topic Different

Lactate is where the acute/chronic distinction that emerged in the [[serotonin]] analysis proves most essential. The mainstream rehabilitation of lactate is built almost entirely on acute physiology: exercise lactate shuttling, acute fuel provision to neurons, acute wound healing signaling. Peat's concern is chronic lactate elevation from sustained respiratory failure. The two literatures study different phenomena, reach different conclusions, and rarely acknowledge each other.

> [!example]- Pattern: Acute/Chronic Distinction
> See [[System Overview#The Acute-Chronic Distinction]] for the framework-wide treatment.

The PNAS finding that high brain lactate is "a hallmark of aging" and that LDH overexpression shortens lifespan while LDH downregulation extends it is the single most direct test of Peat's thesis in the modern literature. Published in one of the most prestigious journals in science, it confirms that chronic lactate production drives aging at the cellular level, not just correlates with it. Yet this finding has not penetrated the "lactate is good" narrative that dominates exercise physiology and popular science communication.

The histone lactylation research is equally striking. Published in 2020-2026, it reveals that lactate doesn't just acidify tissue — it rewrites gene expression in macrophages, driving them toward pro-fibrotic phenotypes through a mechanism (histone modification) that Peat could not have known about. The fibrosis he attributed to lactate is confirmed through a pathway more powerful and specific than anything he described. The research is catching up to his conclusion through mechanisms he didn't predict.

## What This Means in Practice

The core principle: health is efficient oxidative metabolism producing [[co2|CO2]]. Disease is failed oxidative metabolism producing lactate.

**The [[co2|CO2]]/lactate ratio as specific diagnostic.** Rising end-tidal [[co2|CO2]] and falling blood lactate together capture the metabolic state more completely than any other combination of tests. This is the measurable, objective version of what temperature and pulse approximate.

**The Ringer's lactate clinical note.** Bicarbonate-based resuscitation fluids outperform lactate-based fluids for inflammatory suppression in shock. Hospitals resuscitate shock patients with a fluid containing the molecule driving the shock pathology. This is worth knowing.

For the shared intervention framework, see [[Practical Convergence]].
