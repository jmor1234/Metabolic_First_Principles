# Thyroid: The Master Throttle

## Overview

Every document in this series points to thyroid. PUFA suppresses it. Estrogen rises when it's insufficient. Progesterone synthesis depends on it. Serotonin increases when it fails. Cholesterol accumulates when it slows. The gut stagnates when it declines. Yet thyroid itself has not been grounded until now.

Peat positioned thyroid hormone, specifically T3 (triiodothyronine), as the master variable in the metabolic framework. Not one regulator among many. The regulator. It sets the rate at which the entire oxidative system operates by controlling how many copies of each energy-producing enzyme exist in each cell. Everything documented in the estrogen, progesterone, and PUFA analyses either suppresses thyroid function or depends on thyroid function for its own resolution. Without adequate thyroid, no other intervention fully works. With adequate thyroid, many other problems self-correct.

## Claim 1: T3 as the Master Metabolic Regulator

### What Peat Actually Said

T3 is the biologically active thyroid hormone. It determines the rate of oxidative metabolism across all tissues by regulating gene transcription for energy-producing enzymes. "The higher rate of metabolism produced by adequate thyroid function maintains a high rate of renewal of the cell's systems, keeping the cell constantly adjusted to slight changes in the organism's needs."

The mechanism is not indirect. T3 enters cells, mitochondria, and nuclei, where it directly controls the expression of respiratory enzymes. The brain concentrates T3 from serum at six times the serum level while actively expelling T4, indicating that the brain treats T3 as the primary regulatory signal and T4 as a less useful precursor.

"The basal metabolic rate, which is mainly governed by thyroid, roughly corresponds to the average body temperature." This is the observable consequence: temperature is a direct readout of metabolic heat production, which is a direct readout of mitochondrial oxidative activity, which is directly governed by T3.

When T3 is adequate, cells produce energy efficiently. Lactic acid is not produced unnecessarily. Cells maintain proper water balance, proper ion gradients, and the structured "resting" state that Peat associated with health. When T3 is insufficient, all of this degrades: cells swell with water, calcium accumulates, glucose oxidation shifts toward lactic acid production, and the organism enters the stress-adapted state described in every other document in this series.

### The Downstream Cascade

The consequences of thyroid insufficiency are not confined to "metabolism" in the narrow sense. They cascade through every system the framework addresses:

**Cholesterol conversion.** T3 drives the conversion of cholesterol to pregnenolone, the master precursor for all steroid hormones (progesterone, DHEA, cortisol, testosterone, estrogen). "By 1936, it was clear that hypercholesterolemia in humans and other animals was caused by hypothyroidism." When thyroid slows, cholesterol accumulates in blood because it is not being converted to protective steroids. "A rise in the concentration of cholesterol was considered to be one of the most reliable ways to diagnose hypothyroidism" in the 1930s. Thyroid also drives cholesterol conversion to bile acids in the liver, essential for fat digestion and intestinal health.

**Progesterone synthesis.** Since T3 drives cholesterol-to-pregnenolone conversion, hypothyroidism directly reduces progesterone production. Less progesterone means less opposition to estrogen. This connects the thyroid document to the progesterone document: the pregnenolone steal under chronic stress is compounded by hypothyroidism's failure to produce adequate pregnenolone in the first place.

**Estrogen clearance.** The liver clears estrogen from circulation. Liver function depends on metabolic rate. Hypothyroidism impairs liver clearance of estrogen, allowing estrogen to accumulate. So hypothyroidism produces estrogen dominance through two simultaneous pathways: reduced progesterone synthesis and reduced estrogen clearance. This connects to the estrogen document's point about context-dependence: thyroid status determines whether estrogen's metabolic effects are modest or devastating.

**Serotonin regulation.** "Hypothyroidism increases serotonin activity in the body, as it increases estrogen dominance." And: "Carbon dioxide and serotonin are regulated most importantly by thyroid function. Carbon dioxide inhibits the release of serotonin." When thyroid is adequate, CO2 production is high, serotonin is suppressed, and the conservation/torpor physiology stays inactive. When thyroid fails, CO2 drops, serotonin rises, and the organism shifts toward the energy-conserving state documented in the PUFA-serotonin section.

**Heart function.** T3 regulates the calcium-binding proteins (phospholamban, calcisequestrin) that allow heart muscle to relax between contractions. "Thyroid's beneficial effects on heart and skeletal muscle have been known empirically for 100 years." Heart transplant surgeons administer T3 to brain-dead donors to improve organ viability, an acknowledgment of T3's direct cardiac effects even by surgeons who would never prescribe it to living patients with heart failure.

**Nerve function.** Hypothyroidism produces "slowed conduction along nerves, and slowed recovery and readiness for new responses. Slow reaction time is associated with slowed memory, perception, and other mental processes." The Achilles tendon reflex relaxation test, traditionally used to diagnose hypothyroidism, measures exactly this: the speed at which a nerve-muscle system can restore its energized resting state after stimulation.

### The First-Principles Logic

If health is efficient oxidative metabolism (Brick 1 of the framework), and T3 determines the rate of oxidative metabolism by controlling gene transcription for respiratory enzymes, then T3 is the control variable for health. Everything else either feeds into T3 production and activity or flows downstream from it. PUFA, estrogen, serotonin, and cortisol all suppress T3. Progesterone, saturated fat, adequate glucose, and CO2 all support it or depend on it. The entire framework rotates around this axis.

## Claim 2: The Diagnostic Critique — TSH Testing Is Inadequate

### What Peat Actually Said

This is Peat's most practically consequential claim. He argued that modern thyroid diagnosis, built primarily on TSH (thyroid stimulating hormone) measurement, has systematically underdiagnosed hypothyroidism for decades, leaving the majority of hypothyroid people untreated while telling them their thyroid is "normal."

**The historical shift.** Before World War II, hypothyroidism was diagnosed by clinical signs and basal metabolic rate (BMR) measurement — oxygen consumption measured while lying quietly in the morning without having eaten. The estimated prevalence of hypothyroidism in Americans was 30-40%. Between 1940 and 1950, diagnosis shifted to protein-bound iodine (PBI) blood tests, and later to TSH. The estimated prevalence dropped to approximately 5%. Peat argued this represented a redefinition of the disease, not a reduction in its incidence. The biology didn't change. The diagnostic criteria did.

**The TSH range problem.** Peat identified the "normal" TSH range as physiologically arbitrary. "With any other biological measurement, such as erythrocyte count, blood pressure, body weight, or serum sodium, calcium, chloride, or glucose, a variation of ten or 20 percent from the mean is considered to be meaningful." Yet TSH allows 8-10 fold variations within the "normal" range. His clinical observation: "Over a period of several years, I never saw a person whose TSH was over 2 microIU/ml who was comfortably healthy."

**Factors that suppress TSH independently of thyroid status.** "Aging, infection, trauma, prolonged cortisol excess, somatostatin, dopamine or L-dopa, adrenaline, amphetamine, caffeine and fever can lower TSH, apart from the effect of feedback by the thyroid hormones." This creates a situation where TSH can appear normal or low during genuine hypothyroidism. A stressed, cortisol-elevated person with suppressed TSH and inadequate T3 will test as "normal" on a TSH screen while being functionally hypothyroid by every physiological measure.

**TSH as a direct pathogen.** Peat went further than simply calling TSH an unreliable diagnostic marker. He argued TSH itself causes disease through "pro-inflammatory actions." TSH stimulates inflammatory responses in mast cells, fibroblasts, fat cells, and pigment cells. "TSH has direct actions on many cell types other than the thyroid, and probably contributes directly to edema, fibrosis, and mastocytosis." Pituitary-deficient dwarf mice lacking TSH, prolactin, and growth hormone live approximately a year longer than normal mice, suggesting that suppression of TSH (not just adequate thyroid hormone) may itself be health-promoting.

**Clinical misdiagnosis.** Peat described patients who presented with classic hypothyroid symptoms (fatigue, weakness, poor memory, insomnia, low pulse, low temperature) who were diagnosed as hyperthyroid based solely on blood tests and scheduled for thyroid gland destruction. "Simply on the basis of some laboratory tests, they were going to have their thyroid gland destroyed. But on the basis of all of the traditional ways of judging thyroid function, they were hypothyroid."

### The First-Principles Logic

If the organism's metabolic state is the thing that matters (Brick 1), and TSH is a pituitary hormone subject to multiple independent regulatory inputs that have nothing to do with actual thyroid hormone activity in tissues, then TSH is a proxy variable that can diverge from what it's supposed to indicate. A diagnostic system built on a proxy that diverges under stress, aging, infection, and cortisol elevation will systematically miss the population most likely to be hypothyroid: stressed, aging, chronically ill people whose TSH is suppressed by their stress hormones rather than by adequate thyroid hormone.

The framework predicts this misdiagnosis pattern. If the stress-adapted state (Brick 3) suppresses TSH while also suppressing actual thyroid function, then TSH-based diagnosis will selectively fail in exactly the patients who most need treatment. This is not a design flaw that can be fixed with better reference ranges. It is a structural limitation of using a pituitary signal as a surrogate for tissue-level metabolic activity.

## Claim 3: Physiological Markers Are Superior to Blood Tests

### What Peat Actually Said

Peat advocated measuring what thyroid hormone actually does rather than what circulates in blood. The body produces measurable outputs of metabolic rate that are directly observable without a laboratory.

**Temperature.** Basal body temperature approaching 98.6°F reflects adequate metabolic heat production. Broda Barnes pioneered this approach, arguing that axillary temperature before rising was the best diagnostic indicator. Peat acknowledged Barnes' contribution but identified limitations: warm climates, electric blankets, and altitude all confound temperature readings. "In Eugene during a hot and humid summer, I saw several obviously hypothyroid people whose temperature seemed perfectly normal. But I noticed that their pulse rates were, in several cases, very low."

**Pulse.** Resting pulse rate reflects metabolic rate and sympathetic tone. Peat noted that healthy populations average approximately 85 beats per minute at rest, while "less healthy" groups average 70. However, some hypothyroid patients maintain chronically high pulse rates through excessive adrenaline compensation. When supplemented with thyroid, "their pulse rates came down to normal." The paradox: both abnormally low and abnormally high pulse rates can indicate hypothyroidism, depending on the degree of adrenergic compensation.

**The combination.** "The combination of pulse rate and temperature is much better than either one alone." Temperature reflects total heat production. Pulse reflects both metabolic rate and stress hormone activity. Together they distinguish genuine metabolic adequacy (warm, moderate pulse) from stress-compensated hypothyroidism (warm from adrenaline vasoconstriction, high pulse from catecholamine excess) and from decompensated hypothyroidism (cold, low pulse).

**The meal test.** Taking temperature and pulse before and after breakfast reveals the metabolic response to fuel. In healthy people, both rise after eating, because food provides substrate for increased oxidation. "In very hypothyroid people either, or both, might fall." In adrenaline-compensated hypothyroidism, temperature may actually drop after eating, because the rise in blood sugar allows adrenaline and cortisol to fall — the thermogenic contribution from stress hormones was maintaining the pre-breakfast temperature, and breakfast removes the stimulus for their production.

**Carbon dioxide.** Peat argued that CO2 measurement is the most accurate indicator of genuine thyroid-driven oxidative metabolism, because it eliminates confounds from stress hormones. "Oxidation of glucose produces more carbon dioxide than oxidation of fat does." High adrenaline increases fat oxidation (producing less CO2 per oxygen consumed), and PUFA peroxidation "consumes some oxygen, without producing any usable energy or carbon dioxide." End-tidal CO2 should approach 5-6% of expired air when thyroid-driven glucose oxidation is adequate.

**Caloric intake and water evaporation.** "Counting calories achieves approximately the same thing as measuring oxygen consumption." A person maintaining stable weight on 1800 calories cannot be hyperthyroid. Water evaporation tracks metabolic rate: approximately one liter evaporated per 1000 calories metabolized. "A hyperthyroid person will invisibly lose several quarts of water in a day, and a hypothyroid person may evaporate a quart or less."

**The Achilles reflex.** The speed of relaxation of the Achilles tendon reflex directly measures the tissue-level effect of thyroid hormone on nerve-muscle function. Slow relaxation reflects slow restoration of the cellular energy needed for repolarization. This is a direct functional measurement of what T3 does in tissue, not a proxy.

**Complementary blood markers.** Peat did not reject blood testing entirely. He identified markers that reflect thyroid function more reliably than TSH: cholesterol (rises in hypothyroidism because T3 drives its conversion), albumin, glucose, sodium, lactate (rises when glucose oxidation is impaired), total T3, and transthyretin (the carrier protein for both vitamin A and thyroid hormones, sharply decreased by stress).

### The First-Principles Logic

Every physiological marker Peat identified measures the consequence of thyroid hormone activity in tissue, not the concentration of a pituitary signal in blood. Temperature measures metabolic heat. Pulse measures metabolic rate. CO2 measures oxidative efficiency. Reflex speed measures cellular repolarization rate. Lactate measures the completeness of glucose oxidation. Cholesterol measures the rate of steroid hormone synthesis.

These are all downstream readouts of the thing that actually matters: is T3 doing its job in cells? A normal TSH with low temperature, low CO2, slow reflexes, elevated lactate, and high cholesterol describes a person who is functionally hypothyroid regardless of what the pituitary says. The organism is readable (Brick 8 of the framework), and the readings that matter are the ones that measure function, not feedback signals.

## Claim 4: The Anti-Thyroid Environment

### What Peat Actually Said

Peat argued that the modern metabolic environment systematically suppresses thyroid function through multiple converging mechanisms, creating widespread subclinical hypothyroidism that standard testing does not detect. The suppressors are the same substances documented throughout this series.

**PUFA.** Polyunsaturated fatty acids suppress thyroid at multiple points. They [displace T4 from transthyretin](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7488876/), reducing effective transport. They block thyroid hormone production and transport during stress. They "interfere directly with the respiratory functions of the mitochondria," undermining the very process thyroid hormone is supposed to enhance. "Plasma free fatty acids, inhibitor of extrathyroidal conversion of T4 to T3 and thyroid hormone binding inhibitor" have been documented in patients with nonthyroidal illness. The PUFA document established this chain in detail: PUFA suppresses thyroid, thyroid suppression allows estrogen to rise, estrogen increases PUFA retention, and the loop deepens.

**Estrogen.** Estrogen inhibits MAO (monoamine oxidase), increasing serotonin activity, which suppresses thyroid function. Estrogen also increases the liver's production of thyroid-binding globulin, which sequesters thyroid hormone in an inactive bound state. Hypothyroidism in turn impairs liver clearance of estrogen, creating the bidirectional amplification documented in the estrogen analysis.

**Cortisol and stress.** "Cortisol inhibits the conversion of T4 to T3," the metabolically active form. Adrenaline "decreases the conversion to T4 to T3, and increases the formation of the antagonistic reverse T3." Reverse T3 competes with T3 at cellular sites without producing its metabolic effects, further reducing functional thyroid activity. During stress, TSH paradoxically reaches its peak at the same time free fatty acids and adrenaline are highest, and "TSH itself can produce lipolysis, raising the level of circulating free fatty acids. This suggests that a high level of TSH could sometimes contribute to functional hypothyroidism, because of the antimetabolic effects of the unsaturated fatty acids."

**Serotonin.** Serotonin suppresses mitochondrial energy production and causes vasoconstriction, reducing oxygen delivery to tissues. Both effects reduce the metabolic activity that thyroid hormone is supposed to support. "Carbon dioxide inhibits the release of serotonin," and thyroid function determines CO2 production. When thyroid falls, CO2 falls, serotonin rises, and serotonin further suppresses the metabolic activity that would produce CO2. This is another self-amplifying loop.

**Endotoxin.** Bacterial endotoxin from the gut suppresses thyroid function, increases serotonin and estrogen, and impairs liver function. Gut motility, which prevents bacterial overgrowth and endotoxin production, depends on metabolic rate, which depends on thyroid. Hypothyroidism slows the gut, increases endotoxin exposure, and endotoxin further suppresses thyroid. This is the gut-liver-thyroid circuit described in the framework's mapping of circular dependencies.

### The First-Principles Logic

The convergence of all these suppressors on thyroid function explains why hypothyroidism is so common and so self-sustaining. It is not primarily a gland failure. It is a systemic suppression by the same substances (PUFA, estrogen, serotonin, cortisol, endotoxin) that the framework identifies as the mediators of chronic disease. The thyroid gland may be perfectly capable of producing adequate hormone, but if conversion to T3 is blocked by cortisol, transport is impaired by PUFA, activity is opposed by reverse T3, and the downstream metabolic effects are undermined by serotonin and estrogen, the organism is functionally hypothyroid with a "normal" gland.

This is why Peat's framework treats thyroid support not as treatment for a gland disorder but as restoration of the master control variable against systematic environmental suppression. The "treatment" is not just thyroid hormone. It is removing the suppressors: reducing PUFA, opposing estrogen with progesterone, managing stress to reduce cortisol, supporting gut integrity to reduce endotoxin, and providing adequate nutrition (glucose, protein, minerals) to support the metabolic activity that thyroid hormone governs.

## Claim 5: T3 vs T4 in Therapy

### What Peat Actually Said

Most hypothyroid patients are treated with synthetic T4 (levothyroxine/Synthroid) alone. Peat argued this is often insufficient because T4 is a prohormone that requires conversion to T3 in peripheral tissues, and this conversion is impaired by the same factors that caused the hypothyroidism.

**The conversion problem.** Cortisol, adrenaline, PUFA, and stress all inhibit the deiodinase enzymes that convert T4 to T3. They simultaneously increase the production of reverse T3, which competes with T3 at cellular sites. A patient taking T4-only therapy whose conversion is impaired by chronic stress and PUFA-loaded tissues will accumulate T4 and reverse T3 while remaining T3-deficient. Their TSH will normalize (because T4 suppresses TSH), but their tissues will not receive adequate active hormone. "Some critics have said that most physicians are 'treating the TSH,' rather than the patient."

**Natural thyroid ratios.** Healthy individuals maintain a T3:T4 ratio of approximately 1:3, reflecting the thyroid gland's natural output. Desiccated thyroid extract (Armour, NP Thyroid) provides both T3 and T4 in approximately this ratio. Synthetic T4 alone produces a T3:T4 ratio that depends entirely on peripheral conversion. In patients with impaired conversion, this ratio diverges from the physiological norm.

**T3 measurement.** "The actual quantity of T3, the active thyroid hormone, in the blood can be measured with reasonable accuracy, and this single test corresponds better to the metabolic rate and other meaningful biological responses than other standard tests do." Yet T3 is rarely the primary test ordered. TSH dominates standard screening, and T3 is often omitted entirely.

**Dose titration by response.** Peat advocated adjusting thyroid dose by physiological response (temperature, pulse, symptom resolution) rather than by blood test values. "Keeping a 14-day chart of pulse rate and temperature allows you to see whether thyroid treatment is producing the desired response." The daily dose "often has to be increased several times, as the state of stress and the adrenaline and cortisol production decrease," because initial doses partially relieve the hypothyroid compensation without fully restoring metabolic rate. As stress hormones normalize, the previously masked hypothyroidism becomes apparent, requiring further dose adjustment.

**The therapeutic trial.** "A therapeutic trial was the final test of the validity of the diagnosis: if the patient's symptoms disappeared as his temperature and pulse rate and food intake were normalized, the diagnostic hypothesis was confirmed." This is diagnosis by response — the most direct possible test of whether thyroid insufficiency was the problem.

### The First-Principles Logic

If T3 is the active hormone and conversion from T4 is impaired by the same environmental factors that cause hypothyroidism, then T4-only therapy treats the feedback signal (TSH) without necessarily restoring the functional output (tissue T3 activity). This is analogous to the measurement problem identified throughout this series: the thing being measured (TSH suppression by T4) is not the thing that matters (T3-driven oxidative metabolism in tissue).

The framework predicts that patients on T4-only therapy who continue to experience hypothyroid symptoms despite "normal" TSH are not treatment failures or psychosomatic complainers. They are people whose T4 is not being converted to T3 because the conversion machinery is suppressed by the same PUFA, cortisol, and estrogen load that caused their hypothyroidism. The treatment matches the test (TSH normalization) without matching the biology (tissue T3 adequacy).

## Research Verification

### Claim 1: T3 as Master Metabolic Regulator — Confirmed

This is the least controversial claim. It is textbook endocrinology, stated more clearly than textbooks typically state it.

T3 regulates mitochondrial activity and biogenesis by modulating expression of proteins encoded by [both the nuclear and mitochondrial genome](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4543916/). It acts through nuclear thyroid receptors (TRalpha and TRbeta) that are constitutively bound to thyroid hormone response elements, stimulating or inhibiting gene transcription when T3 binds. It also indirectly activates respiratory gene transcription by upregulating intermediate factors (NRF-1, NRF-2, PGC1-alpha). And it acts directly on the mitochondrial genome through a mitochondrial matrix receptor (p43) that [binds to TREs of the mitochondrial DNA and induces transcription of oxidative phosphorylation genes](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11174855/) in parallel with nuclear gene transcription.

T3 controls respiratory enzyme expression at both the nuclear and mitochondrial level simultaneously. This is not one regulatory input among many. It is the coordinating signal that ensures nuclear-encoded and mitochondria-encoded respiratory components are expressed together. Without it, the electron transport chain cannot be assembled at the rate the organism requires.

The connection to metabolic rate is directly confirmed: [increased plasma free T3 is associated with increased resting metabolic rate](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4543916/) and decreased respiratory quotient (indicating a shift toward fat oxidation, though within the framework glucose oxidation at adequate T3 is preferred). [Thyroid hormones are critical in regulating basal metabolic rate and thermoregulation](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK500006/), with an inverse correlation between TSH levels and BMR confirmed across thyroid function states.

The downstream cascade Peat described (cholesterol, progesterone, estrogen clearance, serotonin) is individually confirmed:

**Cholesterol.** Thyroid hormone [increases expression of CYP7A1 (cholesterol 7-alpha-hydroxylase)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6129606/), converting cholesterol into bile acids, and upregulates LDL receptors via SREBP-2. In hypothyroidism, bile acid flow slows, cholesterol secretion into bile decreases markedly, and hepatic cholesterol uptake from circulation falls. The [prevalence of hypothyroidism is 1.4-13% in patients with hyperlipidemia](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12020-023-03420-9), confirming that a substantial fraction of "high cholesterol" cases are undiagnosed thyroid insufficiency. Peat's claim that elevated cholesterol was the original reliable diagnostic marker for hypothyroidism in the 1930s is historically accurate.

**Serotonin-CO2 axis.** Peat's claim that "carbon dioxide and serotonin are regulated most importantly by thyroid function" and that "carbon dioxide inhibits the release of serotonin" is mechanistically supported. Thyroid drives oxidative metabolism, oxidative metabolism produces CO2, CO2 suppresses serotonin. When thyroid fails, this chain reverses. The connection between hypothyroidism and elevated serotonin is documented in the context of [nonthyroidal illness](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK285570/) and psychiatric conditions.

### Claim 2: The Diagnostic Critique — TSH Testing Is Inadequate — Substantially Supported

**The reference range controversy is real and ongoing.** The [National Academy of Clinical Biochemistry has advocated narrowing the upper TSH limit to 2.5 mIU/L](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8453656/), which would reclassify an [additional 9.7% of the US population (20.6 million Americans)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1530891X20431362) as subclinically hypothyroid. Other experts argue for maintaining the wider range. The controversy itself validates Peat's core point: the "normal" range is not settled science. It is an ongoing debate in which the difference between positions represents millions of potentially undertreated people.

**Factors that suppress TSH independently of thyroid status — Confirmed.** This is recognized formally as [euthyroid sick syndrome / nonthyroidal illness syndrome](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK482219/). Approximately 75% of hospitalized patients show thyroid test alterations. The most common pattern is low T3 with low or normal TSH. High cortisol, cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-alpha), and medications (glucocorticoids, dopamine) all suppress TSH independently. In acute hospital settings, [as many as 3% of patients have TSH below 0.1 mU/L attributable to nonthyroidal illness or medication rather than genuine hyperthyroidism](https://joe.bioscientifica.com/view/journals/joe/205/1/1.xml).

The clinical implication is exactly what Peat described: a stressed, sick, cortisol-elevated person with low T3 and suppressed TSH will test as "normal" or even "hyperthyroid" on TSH screening while being functionally hypothyroid by every physiological measure. The medical literature acknowledges this in the hospital setting but does not extend the same logic to outpatients with chronic subclinical stress, PUFA burden, and cortisol elevation — the population Peat argued was most commonly affected.

**TSH as a direct pathogen — Partially supported.** TSH receptors are expressed on [mast cells, fibroblasts, fat cells, and other extrathyroidal tissues](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6449760/). TSH triggers mast cell degranulation and stimulates orbital fibroblast proliferation and glycosaminoglycan synthesis in Graves' orbitopathy. The evidence for TSH as pathogenic in these tissues comes primarily from the Graves' disease literature, where TSH receptor stimulation drives inflammatory orbital disease. Whether baseline TSH at the upper end of "normal" produces clinically meaningful inflammatory effects in healthy tissue is less established. Peat's claim that pituitary-deficient dwarf mice (lacking TSH) live longer is accurate and documented, but the mice also lack growth hormone and prolactin, making it impossible to attribute the longevity specifically to TSH absence.

### Claim 3: Physiological Markers Are Superior — Supported With Acknowledged Limitations

**Temperature correlates with thyroid function.** Before TSH testing existed, [basal metabolic rate measured through temperature was used to diagnose thyroid disease](https://ospublishers.com/Hypothyroidism-The-Underdiagnosed-Metabolic-Disorder.html). An [inverse correlation between TSH and BMR has been confirmed](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/397530822_Evaluating_the_Effect_of_Hypothyroidism_on_Basal_Metabolic_Rate_and_Body_Temperature_Regulation), with significant differences in BMR across thyroid function groups. Decreased core body temperature with hypothyroidism and increased temperature with hyperthyroidism are described as "hallmark clinical features" in standard physiology references.

The mainstream position is that temperature is not sensitive or specific enough to be the "gold standard" for diagnosis. This is likely true as a standalone measure — Peat himself identified the limitations (climate confounds, adrenaline compensation, electric blankets). But the mainstream rejects temperature monitoring largely because TSH testing exists, not because temperature was demonstrated to be inferior in head-to-head comparison with physiological outcomes as the reference standard. The studies that would test "does temperature-guided treatment produce better patient outcomes than TSH-guided treatment" have not been conducted. There is no commercial incentive to conduct them.

**The combination of temperature and pulse is more informative than either alone.** This claim has face validity within physiology (temperature reflects heat production, pulse reflects both metabolic rate and sympathetic tone) but has not been formally validated against TSH in clinical trials. It exists in a space where absence of evidence reflects absence of funding, not absence of plausibility.

**Persistent symptoms despite normal TSH validate the diagnostic critique.** This is where the mainstream literature inadvertently confirms Peat's position most powerfully. [Approximately 15-25% of hypothyroid patients on levothyroxine report persistent symptoms despite normal TSH](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5824691/). These patients report decreased quality of life, fatigue, weight gain, cognitive impairment, and mood changes. The [Endocrine Society has acknowledged](https://www.endocrine.org/news-and-advocacy/news-room/2018/patients-on-levothyroxine-continue-to-experience-symptoms-despite-normal-thyroid-hormone-levels) that "some patients on levothyroxine continue to experience symptoms despite normal thyroid hormone levels."

The mainstream response to this is puzzlement and speculation about non-thyroidal causes. Peat's framework provides a direct explanation: these patients have normalized their TSH (which is what T4 does) without restoring adequate tissue T3 (which requires conversion that may be impaired). They are "treated" by the test and untreated by their biology. The persistent symptoms are not mysterious. They are the predictable consequence of measuring feedback rather than function.

### Claim 4: The Anti-Thyroid Environment — Confirmed Across All Suppressors

**Cortisol inhibits T4 to T3 conversion — Confirmed.** [Glucocorticoids decrease conversion of thyroxine to triiodothyronine](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7053919/) in isolated renal tubules. High cortisol promotes type 3 deiodinase activity (converting T4 to inactive reverse T3) while inhibiting type 1 and type 2 deiodinase (which convert T4 to active T3). This is a well-established mechanism in the endocrine literature, not a contested claim. The body shifts away from active thyroid hormone under stress as an energy conservation response.

**PUFA impairs thyroid transport and conversion — Confirmed.** As documented in the PUFA analysis, [arachidonic acid inhibits T4 binding to transthyretin](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7488876/), and plasma free fatty acids during nonthyroidal illness act as thyroid hormone binding inhibitors. PUFA interference with thyroid function operates at transport (carrier protein displacement), conversion (deiodinase competition), and end-organ (mitochondrial membrane) levels simultaneously.

**The nonthyroidal illness syndrome is itself confirmation of the anti-thyroid environment thesis.** The medical literature describes a condition — [euthyroid sick syndrome](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK482219/) — in which stress, illness, cytokines, cortisol, and free fatty acids suppress T3 while TSH remains normal or low. This is exactly the pattern Peat described: the modern metabolic environment (stress, PUFA, inflammation) creates functional hypothyroidism that TSH testing does not detect. The difference is that the mainstream restricts this diagnosis to acutely hospitalized patients, while Peat argued the same mechanisms operate chronically at lower intensity in the general population.

### Claim 5: T3 vs T4 Therapy — Supported by Emerging Evidence

**T4-only therapy produces lower T3 than endogenous production.** [7.2% of hypothyroid patients on levothyroxine have free T4 above the upper normal limit, and 15.2% have free T3 below the lower normal limit](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8566135/). This means a substantial fraction of "adequately treated" patients have subnormal T3 despite supranormal T4. The T4 is present. The conversion to active T3 is not occurring at the physiological rate. This is precisely the conversion problem Peat identified.

**Patient preference favors combination therapy.** Across blinded trials, [46.2% of patients preferred combination T4/T3 therapy](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8566135/). In studies comparing T4 monotherapy, T4/T3 combination, and desiccated thyroid extract, satisfaction was lowest with T4 alone and highest with desiccated thyroid. [Overall, 52% of patients preferred combination therapy, 24% preferred T4 monotherapy, and 24% had no preference](https://www.thyroid.org/patient-thyroid-information/ct-for-patients/february-2025/vol-18-issue-2-p-5-6/).

**The trial results are mixed — but the context matters.** Meta-analyses generally show "no significant difference" between T4 monotherapy and combination therapy on quality of life measures. However, context is critical here. Most trials [included hypothyroid patients irrespective of persistent symptoms](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8566135/) rather than targeting the subset who are poor converters. If 15-25% of patients have impaired conversion, and a trial enrolls all comers, the signal from the responders is diluted by the 75-85% who convert adequately and don't benefit from added T3. This is exactly the RCT averaging problem described in the framework's epistemological context document: a targeted intervention helping a specific subgroup looks like noise in population data.

**The DIO2 polymorphism identifies the mechanism.** Patients carrying combined MCT10 and DIO2 gene polymorphisms (affecting T4-to-T3 conversion efficiency) [showed preference for combination therapy at rates of 42%, 63%, and 100% respectively](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8566135/) depending on polymorphism count. This directly confirms that impaired conversion is a real, genetically variable trait that determines response to T3 addition. Peat attributed impaired conversion primarily to environmental suppressors (PUFA, cortisol, estrogen). The DIO2 data adds a genetic layer: some people are constitutively poor converters, and these are the people who benefit most from T3. Both mechanisms (genetic and environmental) predict the same clinical observation: a subset of patients who are undertreated by T4 alone.

## What This Means in Practice

Thyroid is the master throttle. The research confirms this at the molecular level (gene transcription for respiratory enzymes), the cellular level (mitochondrial biogenesis), the organ level (heart relaxation, nerve conduction), and the systemic level (cholesterol, temperature, metabolic rate). Peat's description of T3's role is textbook endocrinology stated without euphemism.

The diagnostic critique is the most practically important claim. TSH testing misses a substantial population of functionally hypothyroid people — those whose TSH is suppressed by stress, those in the 2.0-4.5 range that the current system calls "normal," and those on T4 therapy who cannot convert adequately. The mainstream literature acknowledges each of these problems individually (the reference range debate, euthyroid sick syndrome, persistent symptoms on levothyroxine) without connecting them into the unified picture Peat presented: that modern diagnostic and treatment practices systematically underserve the hypothyroid population.

The practical implications for someone working within this framework:

**Monitor function, not feedback.** Temperature, pulse, and their response to meals measure what thyroid hormone is doing in tissue. TSH measures what the pituitary is doing in response to circulating T4. These are different questions with different answers.

**Address the suppressors.** PUFA reduction, stress management, estrogen opposition (progesterone), and gut health (endotoxin reduction) all improve thyroid function by removing the environmental factors that suppress conversion and activity. This connects directly to every other document in the series: the interventions that address PUFA, estrogen, and stress also address thyroid, because thyroid suppression and PUFA/estrogen/stress amplification are the same system viewed from different angles.

**T3 matters.** If thyroid supplementation is indicated, the conversion problem means T4-only therapy may be insufficient. Measuring T3 directly, or monitoring physiological response (temperature, pulse, symptom resolution) to titrate dose, addresses the biology rather than the feedback signal.

**Cholesterol is a readout, not a target.** Elevated cholesterol in the context of hypothyroid signs (low temperature, fatigue, slow reflexes) likely indicates insufficient thyroid-driven cholesterol conversion, not cardiovascular risk requiring statin therapy. Treating the thyroid rather than the cholesterol addresses the cause rather than the downstream marker.

## What Makes This Topic Different

The estrogen, progesterone, and PUFA analyses each identified places where Peat's mechanism was slightly off while his conclusion was correct — the right destination reached by a slightly wrong route. Thyroid is different. There is no mechanistic correction to make. T3 governs respiratory enzyme transcription at both genome levels. Cortisol inhibits T4-to-T3 conversion. PUFA displaces thyroid from its carriers. TSH is suppressed by stress independently of thyroid status. 15.2% of T4-treated patients have subnormal T3. 15-25% of treated patients report persistent symptoms. The DIO2 polymorphism proves conversion impairment is real. Every individual claim is confirmed by the mainstream's own research.

The difference is that the mainstream contains these findings in separate silos. The endocrinologists debating TSH reference ranges do not reference the geneticists studying DIO2 polymorphisms. The critical care physicians managing euthyroid sick syndrome do not connect their findings to the outpatient persistent symptom literature. The researchers documenting 15.2% subnormal T3 on levothyroxine do not cite the reference range studies suggesting 20.6 million additional Americans may be subclinically hypothyroid. Each finding lives in its own journal, its own subspecialty, its own clinical context.

Peat read across all of them and saw one picture: a master metabolic regulator systematically suppressed by the modern environment, inadequately measured by a feedback signal that diverges from tissue reality under stress, and inadequately treated by a prohormone that requires the same suppressed conversion machinery to become active. The institutional structure produces fragments. He produced integration. The research does not need to be reinterpreted to support his position. It needs only to be read together.
