# Thyroid: The Master Throttle

Every document in this series points to thyroid. [[pufa|PUFA]] suppresses it. [[estrogen|Estrogen]] rises when it's insufficient. [[progesterone|Progesterone]] synthesis depends on it. [[serotonin|Serotonin]] increases when it fails. Cholesterol accumulates when it slows. The gut stagnates when it declines.

Peat positioned thyroid hormone, specifically T3 (triiodothyronine), as the master variable in the metabolic framework. Not one regulator among many. The regulator. It sets the rate at which the entire oxidative system operates by controlling how many copies of each energy-producing enzyme exist in each cell. Everything documented in the [[estrogen]], [[progesterone]], and [[pufa|PUFA]] analyses either suppresses thyroid function or depends on thyroid function for its own resolution. Without adequate thyroid, no other intervention fully works. With adequate thyroid, many other problems self-correct.

## Research Context

> [!warning] Diagnostic Infrastructure Lock-In — The Distortion Is Built Into the Standard of Care
> Levothyroxine (synthetic T4) is the [2nd-4th most prescribed drug in the United States](https://www.goodrx.com/synthroid/why-synthroid-is-the-most-prescribed-drug-in-the-us) with [102.6 million prescriptions in 2019](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/2781311) and approximately [23 million Americans](https://medicine.yale.edu/news-article/21-million-americans-may-take-a-hypothyroidism-drug-they-dont-need/) currently taking it. The global levothyroxine market is valued at [$4.4-4.6 billion](https://www.businessresearchinsights.com/market-reports/levothyroxine-market-121143). The diagnostic infrastructure (TSH testing) and the therapeutic infrastructure (T4-only therapy) are mutually reinforcing: TSH is the primary test, T4 normalizes TSH, therefore T4 is the treatment, therefore TSH is the test. Challenging TSH adequacy challenges a diagnostic-therapeutic complex worth billions. Meanwhile, desiccated thyroid extract accounts for [~6% of thyroid medication users](https://www.medfinder.com/blog/armour-thyroid-shortage-what-providers-prescribers-need-to-know-2026) (1.5M vs 22M patients), and the [FDA announced enforcement action against unapproved animal-derived thyroid medications in August 2025](https://www.medscape.com/viewarticle/fda-warns-future-action-animal-derived-thyroid-drugs-2025a1000lqn), potentially removing desiccated thyroid products from the market entirely.

**Who funds the research.** The financial landscape is dominated by levothyroxine. The [$4.4-4.6 billion global market](https://www.businessresearchinsights.com/market-reports/levothyroxine-market-121143) is led by AbbVie (Synthroid brand) with major generic competitors. [North America accounts for ~45.5% of global levothyroxine revenue](https://www.researchnester.com/reports/levothyroxine-sodium-market/6956). Desiccated thyroid (Armour, NP Thyroid) and liothyronine (T3) together represent a tiny fraction of the market. T4-only therapy generates the overwhelming majority of thyroid drug revenue. The research incentive structure reflects this: the vast majority of thyroid treatment trials use levothyroxine as the intervention and TSH as the primary endpoint. There is no commercial sponsor for large trials of desiccated thyroid or T3 combination therapy — the molecules are generic or under regulatory threat.

**What comparison the research makes.** Most hypothyroidism treatment studies compare levothyroxine vs. placebo, or test whether TSH normalization correlates with symptom resolution. The T4+T3 combination therapy question has been studied in [at least 14 randomized trials](https://www.mdpi.com/1422-0067/25/17/9218): 10 showed no superiority of combination therapy for quality-of-life endpoints, 2 showed superiority on multiple measures, and 2 showed superiority on a minority of measures. However, [meta-analysis of patient preference](https://www.thyroid.org/patient-thyroid-information/ct-for-patients/february-2025/vol-18-issue-2-p-5-6/) showed 52% preferred combination therapy, 24% preferred T4 monotherapy, and 24% had no preference. The [DIO2 Thr92Ala polymorphism](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/102/5/1623/2966904), present in [12-30% of the population depending on ethnicity](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11135370/), reduces D2-mediated T4-to-T3 conversion. In patients carrying both DIO2 and MCT10 polymorphisms, [100% preferred combination treatment](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5527224/). The mixed trial results may reflect population heterogeneity: most patients convert T4 adequately; a genetically identifiable subpopulation does not. The ATA/ETA now [support an individualized trial of T3 addition for symptomatic patients on optimized T4](https://etj.bioscientifica.com/view/journals/etj/10/1/ETJ512970.xml).

**What timescale the research covers.** TSH normalizes within weeks of levothyroxine initiation. Symptom resolution takes months. The mismatch between biochemical normalization and clinical improvement is the core complaint of hypothyroid patients on T4-only therapy and the core critique of the "treating the TSH" approach. No trial has measured the timescale of tissue-level metabolic recovery — the restoration of mitochondrial respiratory enzyme expression, normalization of membrane composition, and recovery from the downstream cascade (progesterone synthesis, estrogen clearance, serotonin suppression) that the framework predicts.

**What endpoint the research measures.** TSH is the primary endpoint for diagnosis, treatment initiation, dose adjustment, and monitoring. [Free T4 is the follow-up test for abnormal TSH](https://emedicine.medscape.com/article/122393-workup). Current guidelines explicitly state that [serum total T3 or free T3 should not be used to diagnose hypothyroidism](https://www.endocrinepractice.org/article/S1530-891X(20)43030-7/fulltext). Temperature, pulse, BMR, end-tidal CO2, cholesterol, Achilles reflex time — all the physiological markers Peat advocated — are not part of the standard thyroid workup. The diagnostic system measures a pituitary signal (TSH) and a prohormone (T4) while explicitly excluding measurement of the active hormone (T3) and the functional outputs of thyroid action.

**Disciplinary fragmentation.** Thyroid research spans specialties with dramatically different relationships to T3:

- **Endocrinology** diagnoses by TSH, treats with T4, explicitly excludes T3 from standard workup — frames hypothyroidism as a TSH/T4 problem
- **Cardiac surgery/transplant medicine** administers T3 directly to [brain-dead donors (85% are T3-deficient)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9086582/) to [resuscitate failing hearts and expand the donor pool](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8266553/) — treats T3 as the critical active hormone
- **Critical care** recognizes [non-thyroidal illness syndrome (NTIS)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK285570/) where T3 drops, reverse T3 rises, and TSH may appear normal — acknowledges that TSH can be misleading in illness
- **Psychiatry** studies thyroid-depression links, T3 augmentation in treatment-resistant depression
- **Genetics** studies DIO2 polymorphisms affecting T4-to-T3 conversion

Cardiac surgeons use T3 to save organs while endocrinologists debate whether to prescribe it. Critical care physicians recognize that TSH is unreliable during illness while primary care uses it as the sole screening test. The fragmentation is not just between specialties — it is between what different specialties consider the same molecule to be worth.

**What the modern literature says about the mechanisms the framework identifies.**

*T3 regulates both nuclear and mitochondrial genomes — confirmed through dedicated mitochondrial receptor.* Two [truncated forms of the TR-alpha-1 nuclear receptor (p43 and p28)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18547504/) have been identified in mitochondria. The p43 protein is a [T3-dependent transcription factor of the mitochondrial genome](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10567517/) — stimulating mitochondrial protein synthesis, respiratory chain activity, and mitochondriogenesis. T3 also acts through nuclear receptors to [upregulate NRF-1, NRF-2, and mitochondrial transcription factor-A](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12552316/), enhancing mitochondrial DNA replication and transcription. The c-erbA-alpha gene [simultaneously encodes a nuclear and a mitochondrial T3 receptor](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10567517/), ensuring coordination of both genomes. Peat's claim that T3 controls respiratory enzyme expression "at both the nuclear and mitochondrial genome levels" is precisely confirmed.

*Non-thyroidal illness syndrome validates the diagnostic critique.* In critical illness, [T3 drops, reverse T3 rises, and TSH may appear normal or low](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK285570/) — the most common pattern is low T3 with normal T4 and TSH. The mechanism: [cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α) suppress TSH](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK482219/) while [cortisol inhibits T4-to-T3 conversion and promotes reverse T3 formation](https://joe.bioscientifica.com/view/journals/joe/205/1/1.xml). In acute hospital settings, [~3% of patients show TSH <0.1 mU/L](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK285570/), and in 75% of these, the low TSH is from NTIS or medications, not hyperthyroidism. This is the clinical manifestation of Peat's claim that "aging, infection, trauma, prolonged cortisol excess" can lower TSH independently of thyroid status.

*TSH reference range remains contested.* The NHANES III-based recommendation to [narrow the upper limit to 2.5 mU/L](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1089/thy.2005.15.1035) (Wartofsky, Dickey) has not been adopted by most labs, which retain the 0.5-4.5/5.0 range. The debate is [explicitly acknowledged as unresolved](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrendo.2011.13) in Nature Reviews Endocrinology. TSH shifts higher with age (up to 7.5 mU/L for >70 years in NHANES data), complicating the question of what "normal" means across the lifespan.

*Reverse T3 as a stress marker.* Chronic stress increases cortisol, which [inhibits 5'-deiodinase type 1 and shifts T4 conversion toward reverse T3](https://restorativemedicine.org/journal/peripheral-thyroid-hormone-conversion-and-its-impact-on-tsh-and-metabolic-activity/) instead of T3. Reverse T3 [binds to T3 receptors without producing metabolic effects](https://restorativemedicine.org/journal/peripheral-thyroid-hormone-conversion-and-its-impact-on-tsh-and-metabolic-activity/), competing with active T3. Elevated reverse T3 is an [independent predictor of mortality in critically ill patients](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/euthyroid-sick-syndrome). A patient with normal TSH, normal T4, high reverse T3, and low T3 is functionally hypothyroid at the tissue level while appearing euthyroid on standard testing.

*T3 in cardiac surgery — direct clinical use.* [T3 deficiency is present in 85% of brain-dead donors](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9086582/). T3 administration [resuscitates failing donor hearts](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8266553/), allowing organs initially rejected for transplant to be used. [116 consecutive donors treated with T3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2305461/) showed good immediate cardiac function in all but 3 recipients. T3 [reduces myocardial ischemia-reperfusion injury and promotes cardiac regeneration](https://www.frontierspartnerships.org/journals/transplant-international/articles/10.3389/ti.2023.10742/full). Cardiac surgeons treat T3 as the directly relevant hormone — they do not give T4 and wait for conversion.

*The overtreatment paradox.* A [Yale study estimated 21 million of 23 million Americans taking levothyroxine may not need it](https://medicine.yale.edu/news-article/21-million-americans-may-take-a-hypothyroidism-drug-they-dont-need/), citing seasonal TSH variation causing unnecessary diagnosis. Simultaneously, Peat's framework and the BMR-based historical estimates suggest widespread underdiagnosis of functional hypothyroidism. Both may be true: TSH-based diagnosis produces both false positives (seasonal TSH elevations treated unnecessarily) and false negatives (functionally hypothyroid patients with stress-suppressed TSH told they're normal). The diagnostic tool is noisy in both directions.

## Claim 1: T3 as the Master Metabolic Regulator

### Peat's Position

T3 is the biologically active thyroid hormone. It determines the rate of oxidative metabolism across all tissues by regulating gene transcription for energy-producing enzymes.

> [!quote] Peat
> "The higher rate of metabolism produced by adequate thyroid function maintains a high rate of renewal of the cell's systems, keeping the cell constantly adjusted to slight changes in the organism's needs."

The mechanism is not indirect. T3 enters cells, mitochondria, and nuclei, where it directly controls the expression of respiratory enzymes. The brain concentrates T3 from serum at six times the serum level while actively expelling T4, indicating that the brain treats T3 as the primary regulatory signal and T4 as a less useful precursor.

> [!quote] Peat
> "The basal metabolic rate, which is mainly governed by thyroid, roughly corresponds to the average body temperature."

Temperature is a direct readout of metabolic heat production, which is a direct readout of mitochondrial oxidative activity, which is directly governed by T3.

When T3 is adequate, cells produce energy efficiently. [[lactate|Lactic acid]] is not produced unnecessarily. Cells maintain proper water balance, proper ion gradients, and the structured "resting" state that Peat associated with health. When T3 is insufficient, all of this degrades: cells swell with water, calcium accumulates, glucose oxidation shifts toward lactic acid production, and the organism enters the stress-adapted state described in every other document in this series.

**The downstream cascade** connects thyroid to everything else in the framework:

- **Cholesterol conversion.** T3 drives the conversion of cholesterol to pregnenolone, the master precursor for all steroid hormones ([[progesterone]], DHEA, cortisol, testosterone, [[estrogen]]). When thyroid slows, cholesterol accumulates because it is not being converted to protective steroids. T3 also drives cholesterol conversion to bile acids in the liver, essential for fat digestion and intestinal health. See [[progesterone#Why Progesterone Depletes]].
- **[[progesterone|Progesterone]] synthesis.** Since T3 drives cholesterol-to-pregnenolone conversion, hypothyroidism directly reduces progesterone production. Less progesterone means less opposition to [[estrogen]]. The pregnenolone steal under chronic stress is compounded by hypothyroidism's failure to produce adequate pregnenolone in the first place.
- **[[estrogen|Estrogen]] clearance.** The liver clears estrogen from circulation. Liver function depends on metabolic rate. Hypothyroidism impairs liver clearance of estrogen, allowing estrogen to accumulate. So hypothyroidism produces estrogen dominance through two simultaneous pathways: reduced progesterone synthesis and reduced estrogen clearance.
- **[[serotonin|Serotonin]] regulation.** Hypothyroidism increases serotonin activity by reducing [[co2|CO2]] production. CO2 inhibits serotonin release. When thyroid fails, CO2 drops, serotonin rises, and the organism shifts toward the energy-conserving state documented in [[serotonin#Claim 4]].
- **Heart function.** T3 regulates the calcium-binding proteins (phospholamban, calcisequestrin) that allow heart muscle to relax between contractions. Heart transplant surgeons administer T3 to brain-dead donors to improve organ viability — an acknowledgment of T3's direct cardiac effects even by surgeons who would never prescribe it to living patients with heart failure.
- **Nerve function.** Hypothyroidism produces slowed conduction along nerves, slowed recovery and readiness for new responses, and slowed memory and perception. The Achilles tendon reflex relaxation test measures exactly this: the speed at which a nerve-muscle system can restore its energized resting state after stimulation.

If health is efficient oxidative metabolism, and T3 determines the rate of oxidative metabolism by controlling gene transcription for respiratory enzymes, then T3 is the control variable for health. Everything else either feeds into T3 production and activity or flows downstream from it. [[pufa|PUFA]], [[estrogen]], [[serotonin]], and cortisol all suppress T3. [[progesterone]], saturated fat, adequate glucose, and [[co2|CO2]] all support it or depend on it.

### Verification

> [!success] Confirmed — T3 Dual Genome Regulation, Downstream Cascade, and Cardiac/Neural Effects All Documented

**T3 regulates both nuclear and mitochondrial genomes through a dedicated mitochondrial receptor — confirmed.** The c-erbA-alpha gene [simultaneously encodes a nuclear receptor and a mitochondrial T3 receptor (p43)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10567517/), ensuring coordinated expression of both genomes. p43 is a [T3-dependent transcription factor of the mitochondrial genome](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18547504/) that stimulates mitochondrial protein synthesis, respiratory chain activity, and mitochondriogenesis. Through the nuclear pathway, T3 [upregulates NRF-1, NRF-2, and mitochondrial transcription factor-A (TFAM)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12552316/), enhancing mitochondrial DNA replication and transcription. This is the direct demonstration of Peat's claim: T3 controls how many copies of each energy-producing enzyme exist in each cell, at both genome levels simultaneously.

**Brain T3 — mechanism is local conversion, not direct concentration from serum.** [T4 crosses the BBB preferentially via OATP1C1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4067591/), then is converted to T3 by type-2 deiodinase (D2) in astrocytes. The brain achieves high T3 levels through local enzymatic conversion rather than by "concentrating T3 from serum at six times the serum level." However, in a primate model, [intravenous T3 achieved CSF levels of 3,921 ng/dl vs only 100 ng/dl for T4](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6822088/), suggesting T3 can penetrate when directly provided. The brain's dependence on local conversion makes it vulnerable when D2 activity is impaired — a mechanism relevant to the DIO2 polymorphism discussion in Claim 5.

**T3 drives steroidogenesis — confirmed through mitochondrial energy.** [Thyroidectomy reduced steroidogenic oxygen consumption by ~30%](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0022473182900310) and impaired both phosphorylative oxidation and energy-dependent hydroxylation reactions. T3 maintains the mitochondrial energy production that CYP11A1 requires for cholesterol-to-pregnenolone conversion. Without adequate T3, cholesterol accumulates (not being converted) and steroid hormone synthesis declines — the mechanism documented in [[progesterone#Why Progesterone Depletes]].

**T3 directly controls cardiac relaxation — confirmed through specific calcium-handling proteins.** T3 [upregulates SERCA2a and downregulates phospholamban](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2820695/), enabling efficient calcium reuptake into the sarcoplasmic reticulum during diastole. T3 [reverses ischemia/reperfusion-induced degradation of calcium-handling proteins](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6880411/) and preserves high-energy phosphates. [T3 deficiency is present in 85% of brain-dead donors](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9086582/), and T3 administration directly [resuscitates failing donor hearts](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8266553/). Cardiac surgeons treat T3 as the active hormone — they do not give T4 and wait.

**Achilles reflex relaxation time as T3-dependent nerve function — confirmed with diagnostic accuracy.** [Woltman's sign (delayed reflex relaxation)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2466474/) is [92% predictive for hypothyroidism](https://litfl.com/woltman-sign/), with [100% of hypothyroid patients showing abnormal half-relaxation time](https://litfl.com/woltman-sign/). This measures exactly what Peat described: the speed at which a nerve-muscle system restores its energized resting state — a direct readout of cellular energy status.

## Claim 2: The Diagnostic Critique — TSH Testing Is Inadequate

### Peat's Position

This is Peat's most practically consequential claim. He argued that modern thyroid diagnosis, built primarily on TSH (thyroid stimulating hormone) measurement, has systematically underdiagnosed hypothyroidism for decades, leaving the majority of hypothyroid people untreated while telling them their thyroid is "normal."

**The historical shift.** Before World War II, hypothyroidism was diagnosed by clinical signs and basal metabolic rate (BMR) measurement — oxygen consumption measured while lying quietly in the morning without having eaten. The estimated prevalence of hypothyroidism in Americans was 30-40%. Between 1940 and 1950, diagnosis shifted to protein-bound iodine (PBI) blood tests, and later to TSH. The estimated prevalence dropped to approximately 5%. Peat argued this represented a redefinition of the disease, not a reduction in its incidence. The biology didn't change. The diagnostic criteria did.

**The TSH range problem.** Peat identified the "normal" TSH range as physiologically arbitrary.

> [!quote] Peat
> "With any other biological measurement, such as erythrocyte count, blood pressure, body weight, or serum sodium, calcium, chloride, or glucose, a variation of ten or 20 percent from the mean is considered to be meaningful."

Yet TSH allows 8-10 fold variations within the "normal" range. His clinical observation: "Over a period of several years, I never saw a person whose TSH was over 2 microIU/ml who was comfortably healthy."

**Factors that suppress TSH independently of thyroid status.**

> [!quote] Peat
> "Aging, infection, trauma, prolonged cortisol excess, somatostatin, dopamine or L-dopa, adrenaline, amphetamine, caffeine and fever can lower TSH, apart from the effect of feedback by the thyroid hormones."

This creates a situation where TSH can appear normal or low during genuine hypothyroidism. A stressed, cortisol-elevated person with suppressed TSH and inadequate T3 will test as "normal" on a TSH screen while being functionally hypothyroid by every physiological measure.

**TSH as a direct pathogen.** Peat went further than simply calling TSH an unreliable diagnostic marker. He argued TSH itself causes disease through pro-inflammatory actions. TSH stimulates inflammatory responses in mast cells, fibroblasts, fat cells, and pigment cells. Pituitary-deficient dwarf mice lacking TSH, prolactin, and growth hormone live approximately a year longer than normal mice, suggesting that suppression of TSH (not just adequate thyroid hormone) may itself be health-promoting.

**Clinical misdiagnosis.** Peat described patients who presented with classic hypothyroid symptoms (fatigue, weakness, poor memory, insomnia, low pulse, low temperature) who were diagnosed as hyperthyroid based solely on blood tests and scheduled for thyroid gland destruction.

> [!quote] Peat
> "Simply on the basis of some laboratory tests, they were going to have their thyroid gland destroyed. But on the basis of all of the traditional ways of judging thyroid function, they were hypothyroid."

If the organism's metabolic state is the thing that matters, and TSH is a pituitary hormone subject to multiple independent regulatory inputs that have nothing to do with actual thyroid hormone activity in tissues, then TSH is a proxy variable that can diverge from what it's supposed to indicate. A diagnostic system built on a proxy that diverges under stress, aging, infection, and cortisol elevation will systematically miss the population most likely to be hypothyroid: stressed, aging, chronically ill people whose TSH is suppressed by their stress hormones rather than by adequate thyroid hormone.

### Verification

> [!tip] Confirmed With Nuance — TSH Is Unreliable Under Specific, Well-Documented Conditions; the 30-40% Prevalence Claim Is Unsupported by Modern Validation

**Non-thyroidal illness syndrome confirms TSH unreliability during illness — confirmed.** In critical illness, [T3 drops, reverse T3 rises, and TSH appears normal or low](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK285570/). [Cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α) directly suppress TSH](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK482219/), and [cortisol inhibits T4→T3 conversion while promoting reverse T3 formation](https://joe.bioscientifica.com/view/journals/joe/205/1/1.xml). In acute hospital settings, [~3% of patients show TSH <0.1 mU/L](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK285570/), and in 75% of these, the low TSH results from NTIS or medications, not hyperthyroidism. Peat's list of factors that suppress TSH independently of thyroid status (aging, infection, trauma, cortisol, dopamine) is documented in the NTIS literature.

**TSH reference range debate is real and unresolved.** The NHANES III-derived recommendation to [narrow the upper limit to 2.5 mU/L](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1089/thy.2005.15.1035) (Wartofsky, Dickey, NACB) has not been adopted by most labs, which retain 0.5-4.5/5.0. The debate is [explicitly acknowledged as unresolved in Nature Reviews Endocrinology](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrendo.2011.13). Peat's observation about 8-10 fold variation within "normal" is accurate: a TSH of 0.5 and 4.5 are both "normal" despite a 9-fold difference. TSH also [shifts higher with age](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrendo.2011.13), further complicating interpretation.

**TSH has direct pro-inflammatory effects — partially confirmed.** TSH [increases mast cell populations and triggers degranulation](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6449760/) (confirmed in [[histamine#Claim 2]]). TSH [induces IL-6 expression in fibrocytes and orbital fibroblasts](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0075100), contributing to the pathology of Graves' orbitopathy. TSH's direct inflammatory actions beyond the thyroid gland are documented, though whether TSH itself is broadly pathogenic remains less established than Peat implied.

**Pituitary-deficient dwarf mice longevity — attributed primarily to GH, not TSH.** Ames and Snell dwarf mice lack TSH, prolactin, and growth hormone. Their extended longevity is [primarily attributed to GH deficiency](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/acel.12382) rather than TSH deficiency specifically. Peat's implication that TSH suppression itself promotes longevity is not separable from the GH effect in these models.

**The 30-40% prevalence estimate is unsupported by validated studies.** Barnes argued that [hypothyroidism affects >40% of the population](https://ospublishers.com/Hypothyroidism-The-Underdiagnosed-Metabolic-Disorder.html) based on basal temperature measurements. This estimate has [never been validated by the medical community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Broda_Otto_Barnes) and runs counter to current prevalence estimates (~5% clinical, 3-15% subclinical). The historical shift from BMR-based to TSH-based diagnosis did redefine the disease, but whether the pre-TSH estimates were more accurate or simply less specific is genuinely unknown.

**The overtreatment paradox validates bidirectional noise.** A [Yale study estimated 21 million of 23 million Americans on levothyroxine may not need it](https://medicine.yale.edu/news-article/21-million-americans-may-take-a-hypothyroidism-drug-they-dont-need/) due to seasonal TSH variation causing false diagnosis. Simultaneously, [10-25% of treated patients have persistent symptoms despite normal TSH](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/cen.14473). TSH produces both false positives and false negatives — overdiagnosing some while underdiagnosing others. Peat's critique that TSH is unreliable is supported; his implied conclusion that underdiagnosis vastly dominates is only half the picture.

> [!example]- Pattern: Diagnostic Measurement Divergence
> TSH measures a pituitary signal that can be suppressed by cortisol, cytokines, dopamine, aging, and illness independently of actual thyroid hormone activity in tissues. NTIS demonstrates this clinically: sick patients with low T3 and normal TSH are functionally hypothyroid by every physiological measure while appearing euthyroid on the standard test. See [[System Overview#Diagnostic Measurement Divergence]].

## Claim 3: Physiological Markers Are Superior to Blood Tests

### Peat's Position

Peat advocated measuring what thyroid hormone actually does rather than what circulates in blood. The body produces measurable outputs of metabolic rate that are directly observable without a laboratory.

**Temperature.** Basal body temperature approaching 98.6F reflects adequate metabolic heat production. Broda Barnes pioneered this approach. Peat acknowledged Barnes' contribution but identified limitations: warm climates, electric blankets, and altitude all confound temperature readings.

> [!quote] Peat
> "In Eugene during a hot and humid summer, I saw several obviously hypothyroid people whose temperature seemed perfectly normal. But I noticed that their pulse rates were, in several cases, very low."

**Pulse.** Resting pulse rate reflects metabolic rate and sympathetic tone. Peat noted that healthy populations average approximately 85 beats per minute at rest, while "less healthy" groups average 70. However, some hypothyroid patients maintain chronically high pulse rates through excessive adrenaline compensation. When supplemented with thyroid, "their pulse rates came down to normal." The paradox: both abnormally low and abnormally high pulse rates can indicate hypothyroidism, depending on the degree of adrenergic compensation.

**The combination.**

> [!quote] Peat
> "The combination of pulse rate and temperature is much better than either one alone."

Temperature reflects total heat production. Pulse reflects both metabolic rate and stress hormone activity. Together they distinguish genuine metabolic adequacy (warm, moderate pulse) from stress-compensated hypothyroidism (warm from adrenaline vasoconstriction, high pulse from catecholamine excess) and from decompensated hypothyroidism (cold, low pulse).

**The meal test.** Taking temperature and pulse before and after breakfast reveals the metabolic response to fuel. In healthy people, both rise after eating, because food provides substrate for increased oxidation. In very hypothyroid people either, or both, might fall. In adrenaline-compensated hypothyroidism, temperature may actually drop after eating, because the rise in blood sugar allows adrenaline and cortisol to fall — the thermogenic contribution from stress hormones was maintaining the pre-breakfast temperature, and breakfast removes the stimulus for their production.

**Carbon dioxide.** Peat argued that [[co2|CO2]] measurement is the most accurate indicator of genuine thyroid-driven oxidative metabolism, because it eliminates confounds from stress hormones. High adrenaline increases fat oxidation (producing less CO2 per oxygen consumed), and [[pufa|PUFA]] peroxidation consumes some oxygen without producing any usable energy or carbon dioxide. End-tidal CO2 should approach 5-6% of expired air when thyroid-driven glucose oxidation is adequate.

**Complementary blood markers.** Peat did not reject blood testing entirely. He identified markers that reflect thyroid function more reliably than TSH: cholesterol (rises in hypothyroidism because T3 drives its conversion), albumin, glucose, sodium, [[lactate]] (rises when glucose oxidation is impaired), total T3, and transthyretin (the carrier protein for both vitamin A and thyroid hormones, sharply decreased by stress).

Every physiological marker Peat identified measures the consequence of thyroid hormone activity in tissue, not the concentration of a pituitary signal in blood. A normal TSH with low temperature, low [[co2|CO2]], slow reflexes, elevated [[lactate]], and high cholesterol describes a person who is functionally hypothyroid regardless of what the pituitary says.

### Verification

> [!tip] Confirmed With Nuance — Individual Markers Have Supporting Evidence; the Combination Has Not Been Formally Validated as a Diagnostic Protocol

**Temperature tracks thyroid status — confirmed as physiological principle, not validated as diagnostic test.** Core body temperature reflects metabolic heat production, which is [directly governed by thyroid hormone activation of mitochondrial uncoupling proteins](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK537039/). Progesterone's thermogenic effect ([0.3-0.7°C luteal phase rise](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7575238/), confirmed in [[progesterone#Claim 2]]) demonstrates temperature's sensitivity to metabolic hormones. However, the Barnes basal temperature test has [never been formally validated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Broda_Otto_Barnes) in controlled diagnostic studies. It is described as "an excellent, although not a totally accurate, indicator" — sensitive but not specific.

**Pulse rate correlates with thyroid function — confirmed.** [Heart rate parameters track directly with free T4 levels](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8566120/) in hypothyroid patients monitored by wearable devices. T3 [increases heart rate through positive chronotropic effects](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4318631/) by upregulating beta-adrenergic receptors and ion channels. Both abnormally low pulse (decompensated hypothyroidism) and abnormally high pulse (adrenaline-compensated hypothyroidism) can reflect inadequate T3, as Peat described. The mainstream normal range (60-100 BPM) is very wide; Peat's claim that ~85 BPM reflects healthy metabolic rate is not directly supported by population studies, which report [60-65 BPM as a healthy resting rate](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5462638/).

**End-tidal CO2 is lower in hypothyroidism — confirmed directly.** [ETCO2 levels are lower in both subclinical and overt hypothyroidism](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3056328/) than in healthy controls. Critically, [ETCO2 showed no relationship to TSH or free T4 levels](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3056328/) — confirming both Peat's claim that CO2 reflects metabolic state and his critique that blood thyroid markers don't capture functional status. This is direct evidence that a physiological marker (CO2) detects a metabolic deficit that the standard blood test (TSH/fT4) does not correlate with.

**Achilles reflex relaxation time — historically validated, now replaced.** [92% predictive for hypothyroidism, 100% sensitivity](https://litfl.com/woltman-sign/) (see Claim 1 verification). This was used diagnostically before TSH testing displaced it. It measures tissue-level energy status directly — the speed of cellular re-energization after stimulation.

**Cholesterol as thyroid marker — confirmed mechanism.** T3 drives cholesterol conversion to pregnenolone and bile acids. [Hypothyroidism reliably elevates serum cholesterol](https://www.aafp.org/pubs/afp/issues/2021/0515/p605.html) because the conversion pathway slows. Cholesterol levels reflect thyroid activity at a metabolic level that TSH does not capture.

**The meal test — no formal validation found.** The logic is physiologically sound (food provides substrate for oxidation; if thyroid is adequate, metabolic rate and temperature should rise after eating). No controlled study validating this specific protocol was identified.

**The combination approach — logically sound, not formally tested.** Peat's argument that temperature + pulse together discriminate better than either alone (distinguishing genuine adequacy from adrenaline compensation from decompensation) is physiologically logical. No study has formally tested the combined diagnostic accuracy of temperature + pulse + CO2 against TSH-based diagnosis.

## Claim 4: The Anti-Thyroid Environment

### Peat's Position

Peat argued that the modern metabolic environment systematically suppresses thyroid function through multiple converging mechanisms, creating widespread subclinical hypothyroidism that standard testing does not detect. The suppressors are the same substances documented throughout this series.

**[[pufa|PUFA]]** suppresses thyroid at multiple levels — displacing T4 from transthyretin, blocking T4-to-T3 conversion, and impairing mitochondrial function directly. The PUFA-thyroid-estrogen loop deepens itself: PUFA suppresses thyroid, thyroid suppression allows [[estrogen]] to rise, estrogen increases PUFA retention. See [[pufa#PUFA Displaces Thyroid Hormone]].

**[[estrogen|Estrogen]]** inhibits MAO, increasing [[serotonin]] activity, which suppresses thyroid function. Estrogen also increases the liver's production of thyroid-binding globulin, sequestering thyroid hormone in an inactive bound state. Hypothyroidism in turn impairs liver clearance of estrogen — bidirectional amplification. See [[estrogen]].

**Cortisol and stress** inhibit the conversion of T4 to T3 and increase the formation of antagonistic reverse T3, which competes with T3 at cellular sites without producing metabolic effects. TSH paradoxically peaks when free fatty acids and adrenaline are highest, and TSH itself can produce lipolysis, raising circulating free fatty acids — a high TSH could contribute to functional hypothyroidism through the antimetabolic effects of unsaturated fatty acids.

**[[serotonin|Serotonin]]** suppresses mitochondrial energy production and causes vasoconstriction, reducing oxygen delivery to tissues. [[co2|CO2]] inhibits serotonin release, and thyroid function determines CO2 production. When thyroid falls, CO2 falls, serotonin rises, and serotonin further suppresses the metabolic activity that would produce CO2 — another self-amplifying loop. See [[serotonin#Claim 4]].

**Endotoxin** from the gut suppresses thyroid function, increases serotonin and estrogen, and impairs liver function. Gut motility depends on metabolic rate, which depends on thyroid. Hypothyroidism slows the gut, increases endotoxin exposure, and endotoxin further suppresses thyroid — the gut-liver-thyroid circuit.

The convergence of all these suppressors explains why hypothyroidism is so common and so self-sustaining. It is not primarily a gland failure. It is systemic suppression by the same substances ([[pufa|PUFA]], [[estrogen]], [[serotonin]], cortisol, endotoxin) that the framework identifies as the mediators of chronic disease. The "treatment" is not just thyroid hormone. It is removing the suppressors.

### Verification

> [!success] Confirmed — Each Suppressor Independently Documented; the Convergence Is the Framework's Contribution

**PUFA suppresses thyroid — confirmed with nuance.** [Unsaturated NEFAs inhibit T4 binding to thyroxine-binding globulin and transthyretin](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7488876/), though the effect is [attenuated in vivo by albumin binding](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7488876/) (documented in [[pufa#PUFA Displaces Thyroid Hormone]]). The in vitro mechanism is confirmed; the in vivo magnitude under normal conditions is smaller than laboratory data suggests.

**Estrogen opposes thyroid through multiple pathways — confirmed.** Estrogen [inhibits MAO-A by 28% in the hypothalamus](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9698044/), increasing serotonin (documented in [[estrogen#Claim 5]]). Estrogen increases hepatic production of thyroxine-binding globulin, sequestering thyroid hormone. Hypothyroidism impairs liver clearance of estrogen — bidirectional amplification confirmed.

**Cortisol inhibits T4→T3 conversion and promotes reverse T3 — confirmed.** [Cortisol suppresses TSH, impairs T4-to-T3 conversion via D1 inhibition, and shifts conversion toward reverse T3](https://joe.bioscientifica.com/view/journals/joe/205/1/1.xml). In NTIS, [elevated cortisol contributes to decreased serum T3](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK285570/). Reverse T3 is an [independent predictor of mortality in critically ill patients](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/euthyroid-sick-syndrome).

**Endotoxin directly suppresses thyroid function — confirmed.** [Intravenous LPS in healthy subjects reproduced NTIS changes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0739724098000320): reduced T3, T4, and TSH with increased reverse T3. LPS [alters deiodinase activity](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12171323/) (inhibits D1, upregulates D2 in hypothalamus) and [disrupts thyroid cell function through TLR4-NF-κB pathway](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10884059/). The bidirectional relationship is confirmed: [hypothyroid patients show elevated LPS](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/microbiology/articles/10.3389/fmicb.2025.1661211/full) due to impaired gut motility and barrier integrity, and LPS further suppresses thyroid function — the gut-liver-thyroid circuit Peat described.

**The convergence of suppressors — each confirmed, integration untested.** PUFA, estrogen, cortisol, serotonin, and endotoxin each suppress thyroid function through independently documented mechanisms. The self-amplifying nature of their convergence (each one worsening the conditions that activate the others) is the framework's prediction. No study has tested whether simultaneously addressing multiple suppressors produces greater thyroid recovery than addressing any single one.

> [!example]- Pattern: Fragmentation as the Default Finding
> The evidence for each thyroid suppressor exists in separate literatures — lipid biochemistry for PUFA, reproductive endocrinology for estrogen, stress physiology for cortisol, gastroenterology for endotoxin. No single discipline integrates across them to ask: what is the combined suppressive load? See [[System Overview#Fragmentation as the Default Finding]].

## Claim 5: T3 vs T4 in Therapy

### Peat's Position

Most hypothyroid patients are treated with synthetic T4 (levothyroxine/Synthroid) alone. Peat argued this is often insufficient because T4 is a prohormone that requires conversion to T3 in peripheral tissues, and this conversion is impaired by the same factors that caused the hypothyroidism.

**The conversion problem.** Cortisol, adrenaline, [[pufa|PUFA]], and stress all inhibit the deiodinase enzymes that convert T4 to T3. They simultaneously increase the production of reverse T3, which competes with T3 at cellular sites. A patient taking T4-only therapy whose conversion is impaired by chronic stress and PUFA-loaded tissues will accumulate T4 and reverse T3 while remaining T3-deficient. Their TSH will normalize (because T4 suppresses TSH), but their tissues will not receive adequate active hormone.

> [!quote] Peat
> "Some critics have said that most physicians are 'treating the TSH,' rather than the patient."

**Natural thyroid ratios.** Healthy individuals maintain a T3:T4 ratio of approximately 1:3, reflecting the thyroid gland's natural output. Desiccated thyroid extract (Armour, NP Thyroid) provides both T3 and T4 in approximately this ratio. Synthetic T4 alone produces a T3:T4 ratio that depends entirely on peripheral conversion. In patients with impaired conversion, this ratio diverges from the physiological norm.

**T3 measurement.**

> [!quote] Peat
> "The actual quantity of T3, the active thyroid hormone, in the blood can be measured with reasonable accuracy, and this single test corresponds better to the metabolic rate and other meaningful biological responses than other standard tests do."

Yet T3 is rarely the primary test ordered. TSH dominates standard screening, and T3 is often omitted entirely.

**Dose titration by response.** Peat advocated adjusting thyroid dose by physiological response (temperature, pulse, symptom resolution) rather than by blood test values. The daily dose often has to be increased several times, as the state of stress and the adrenaline and cortisol production decrease, because initial doses partially relieve the hypothyroid compensation without fully restoring metabolic rate. As stress hormones normalize, the previously masked hypothyroidism becomes apparent, requiring further dose adjustment.

**The therapeutic trial.**

> [!quote] Peat
> "A therapeutic trial was the final test of the validity of the diagnosis: if the patient's symptoms disappeared as his temperature and pulse rate and food intake were normalized, the diagnostic hypothesis was confirmed."

This is diagnosis by response — the most direct possible test of whether thyroid insufficiency was the problem.

If T3 is the active hormone and conversion from T4 is impaired by the same environmental factors that cause hypothyroidism, then T4-only therapy treats the feedback signal (TSH) without necessarily restoring the functional output (tissue T3 activity). The framework predicts that patients on T4-only therapy who continue to experience hypothyroid symptoms despite "normal" TSH are not treatment failures or psychosomatic complainers. They are people whose T4 is not being converted to T3 because the conversion machinery is suppressed by the same [[pufa|PUFA]], cortisol, and [[estrogen]] load that caused their hypothyroidism.

### Verification

> [!tip] Confirmed With Nuance — T4-Only Therapy Is Insufficient for a Genetically Identifiable Subpopulation; Mainstream Guidelines Now Acknowledge This

**The thyroid produces ~90% T4 and ~10% T3 — verified.** The thyroid gland secretes [approximately 85 mcg T4 and 6.5 mcg T3 daily](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK537039/), a T4:T3 ratio of ~13:1. The remaining ~26.5 mcg of daily T3 production (~80% of total) comes from [peripheral deiodination of T4 in tissues](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK537039/). Peat's reference to a "T3:T4 ratio of approximately 1:3" likely refers to the serum ratio in healthy individuals, not the secretion ratio.

**10-25% of treated patients have persistent symptoms despite normal TSH — confirmed.** [10-15% do not regain well-being](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/cen.14473) on levothyroxine. In a UK study, [40.3% of treated patients had hypothyroid symptoms vs 24.9% in controls](https://www.endocrine.org/news-and-advocacy/news-room/2018/patients-on-levothyroxine-continue-to-experience-symptoms-despite-normal-thyroid-hormone-levels). Approximately [1 in 4 patients on levothyroxine still reports symptoms](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10686697/) despite normal TSH. This is the clinical manifestation of Peat's critique: normalizing the pituitary signal does not guarantee adequate T3 in tissues.

**DIO2 polymorphism identifies the subpopulation — confirmed.** The [DIO2 Thr92Ala variant (12-30% prevalence)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11135370/) [reduces D2-mediated T4-to-T3 conversion and lowers serum T3](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/102/5/1623/2966904) in thyroid-deficient patients. In patients carrying both [DIO2 and MCT10 polymorphisms, 100% preferred combination T4+T3 treatment](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5527224/). The mixed results of combination therapy trials may reflect population heterogeneity: most patients convert adequately; a genetically identifiable minority does not. Peat's estimate of "15-25% with impaired conversion" aligns with DIO2 variant prevalence.

**Combination therapy trials show mixed results overall but patient preference favors combination.** [14 randomized trials](https://www.mdpi.com/1422-0067/25/17/9218): 10 showed no superiority, 4 showed some superiority. But [meta-analysis of patient preference: 52% preferred combination, 24% T4 monotherapy, 24% no preference](https://www.thyroid.org/patient-thyroid-information/ct-for-patients/february-2025/vol-18-issue-2-p-5-6/). ATA/ETA now [support an individualized trial of T3 for symptomatic patients on optimized T4](https://etj.bioscientifica.com/view/journals/etj/10/1/ETJ512970.xml). This represents a significant shift from the previous stance of T4-only for all patients.

**Reverse T3 accumulates when conversion is impaired — confirmed.** Cortisol [inhibits D1 and shifts conversion toward reverse T3](https://joe.bioscientifica.com/view/journals/joe/205/1/1.xml), which [binds T3 receptors without producing metabolic effects](https://restorativemedicine.org/journal/peripheral-thyroid-hormone-conversion-and-its-impact-on-tsh-and-metabolic-activity/). A patient with normal TSH, normal T4, elevated reverse T3, and low T3 is functionally hypothyroid at the tissue level while appearing euthyroid on standard testing. TSH will normalize because T4 (and reverse T3) suppress TSH, even though the metabolically active hormone (T3) is deficient.

**T3 is used directly in cardiac surgery — confirmed (see Claim 1).** Cardiac surgeons do not give T4 and wait for conversion. They [administer T3 directly](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9086582/) because the clinical situation requires the active hormone immediately. This is a de facto acknowledgment that T4-to-T3 conversion cannot be assumed to work when it matters most.

**Dose titration by physiological response — no formal validation but logically consistent.** Peat's advocacy for adjusting dose by temperature, pulse, and symptom resolution rather than by blood test values has not been formally tested against TSH-guided titration. The [ETCO2 finding](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3056328/) (lower in hypothyroidism, no correlation with TSH/fT4) suggests that functional markers may detect metabolic deficits that blood markers miss. The principle is physiologically sound; the protocol lacks formal validation.

> [!example]- Pattern: Chemical Conflation (of a different kind)
> The conflation here is not between two molecules but between a prohormone (T4) and the active hormone (T3). Clinical guidelines treat T4 replacement as equivalent to thyroid replacement, when ~80% of T3 production depends on peripheral conversion that can be impaired by stress, cortisol, PUFA, and genetic variants. The patient receives the precursor, the lab test normalizes, but the active product may be deficient. See [[System Overview#Chemical Conflation]].

---

> [!abstract] Overall Assessment
> T3's role as the master metabolic regulator is confirmed at every level: dual genome regulation through dedicated nuclear and mitochondrial receptors, downstream cascade to steroidogenesis and estrogen clearance, direct cardiac calcium handling, and nerve function measurable by reflex timing. The diagnostic critique is substantially confirmed: TSH is unreliable during stress, illness, and aging (NTIS); the reference range debate is acknowledged but unresolved; and ETCO2 detects a metabolic deficit in hypothyroidism that TSH/fT4 does not correlate with. The anti-thyroid environment claim is confirmed for each individual suppressor (PUFA, estrogen, cortisol, endotoxin) through independent literatures. The T3/T4 therapy question is the most nuanced: combination therapy trials show mixed results overall, but a genetically identifiable subpopulation (DIO2 Thr92Ala, 12-30%) has impaired conversion and clearly benefits — explaining why trials in unselected populations show average null effects while individual patients respond dramatically. The overtreatment paradox (21M potentially unnecessary prescriptions while 10-25% of treated patients remain symptomatic) demonstrates that TSH-based diagnosis produces noise in both directions. Barnes' 30-40% prevalence estimate is unsupported by modern validation. Physiological markers are individually supported but their combination as a diagnostic protocol has not been formally tested.

## What Makes This Topic Different

Thyroid is the topic where the [[System Overview#Diagnostic Measurement Divergence|diagnostic measurement divergence]] has the greatest clinical impact. Other topics have blind spots — tissue estrogen unmeasured, tissue progesterone effects ignored. But thyroid's diagnostic measurement divergence directly determines whether millions of people receive treatment. The standard of care — TSH → levothyroxine → TSH normalization — creates a closed loop that doesn't test whether tissues received adequate T3. The ETCO2 finding is the most striking evidence: a functional marker detects hypothyroid metabolic deficit while showing no correlation with the blood tests used to diagnose and monitor the condition.

The overtreatment paradox is also unique to this topic. No other topic in the vault has a diagnostic tool that simultaneously produces massive overtreatment (21M potentially unnecessary prescriptions from seasonal TSH variation) and systematic undertreatment (10-25% of treated patients with persistent symptoms, plus an unknown number of functionally hypothyroid people with stress-suppressed TSH who are told they're normal). The same test, the same threshold, generates errors in both directions.

The FDA's 2025 enforcement action against desiccated thyroid products adds a regulatory dimension absent from other topics. If Armour and NP Thyroid are removed from the market, the patients who respond better to combination T3/T4 therapy (including the 12-30% with DIO2 variants) will lose access to their preferred treatment. The regulatory infrastructure may actively narrow therapeutic options at the moment the evidence base is widening to support them.

## Practical Implications

For the shared intervention framework, see [[Practical Convergence]].

**The DIO2 polymorphism has direct therapeutic implications.** [12-30% of the population carries Thr92Ala](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11135370/), which impairs T4→T3 conversion. For these patients, T4-only therapy may be inherently insufficient regardless of dose optimization. Genetic testing for DIO2 variants before initiating thyroid therapy could identify patients who need T3 from the start. The ATA/ETA now [support individualized T3 trials](https://etj.bioscientifica.com/view/journals/etj/10/1/ETJ512970.xml) for symptomatic patients — the clinical guidance is moving toward Peat's position, slowly.

**Physiological monitoring complements blood testing — it does not replace it.** Temperature + pulse + symptom tracking provide real-time functional readouts that detect metabolic state. ETCO2 is the most direct single measure. These should be used alongside (not instead of) TSH/fT4/fT3 to identify the mismatch between biochemical and functional status that affects 10-25% of treated patients.

**Address the suppressors, not just the hormone.** PUFA, estrogen, cortisol, and endotoxin each independently suppress thyroid function. Supplementing thyroid hormone without addressing these suppressors forces the patient into an ongoing pharmacological battle against their own metabolic environment. This is why [[Practical Convergence]] targets multiple inputs simultaneously.
